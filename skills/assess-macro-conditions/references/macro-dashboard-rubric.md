# Macro Dashboard Rubric

## Core Question

Assess a country's economic and market conditions by reading a dashboard across six pillars:

1. Growth and activity.
2. Inflation, labor, and slack.
3. Rates and yield curve.
4. Debt and fiscal position.
5. External, FX, and reserves.
6. Markets and valuation.

Use levels, direction, acceleration, and consistency across indicators. A single chart is rarely enough.

## Indicator Checklist

Use the following indicators where available:

- Real growth versus potential growth.
- Inflation.
- Industrial production.
- Unemployment rate.
- Output gap, preferably central bank or official estimate.
- Short rate and long rate.
- Yield curve.
- Net domestic debt as percent of GDP, preferably 12-month moving average.
- Real FX versus trade-weighted index.
- Reserve flow as percent of GDP.
- Current account as percent of GDP.
- Fiscal balance as percent of GDP.
- Currency index, indexed to start.
- Bond total returns, indexed to start.
- Equity total returns, indexed to start.
- P/E ratio on actual earnings.

## Pillar 1: Growth And Activity

Indicators:
- Real growth.
- Potential growth.
- Industrial production.

Supportive:
- Real growth is above potential or improving toward potential.
- Industrial production is rising steadily.
- Growth is broad and not solely inventory, commodity, or debt-fueled.

Neutral:
- Real growth is near potential.
- Industrial production is flat or mixed.
- Growth has temporary distortions but no clear stress.

Stressed:
- Real growth is below potential, falling, or volatile.
- Industrial production contracts or sharply decelerates.
- Growth is strong but clearly unsustainable because it depends on excessive credit, external borrowing, or asset speculation.

Read-through:
- Strong growth with stable inflation and debt is healthy.
- Strong growth with a closing or positive output gap, rising inflation, rising rates, and asset excess is late-cycle or overheating.
- Weak growth with falling inflation and rising unemployment is slowdown or recession pressure.

## Pillar 2: Inflation, Labor, And Slack

Indicators:
- Inflation.
- Unemployment rate.
- Output gap.

Supportive:
- Inflation is positive and stable near the country's target or norm.
- Unemployment is falling without clear wage/price instability.
- Output gap is near zero or closing from negative territory.

Neutral:
- Inflation is modestly high or low but moving in the right direction.
- Unemployment is stable.
- Output gap is mildly positive or negative.

Stressed:
- Inflation accelerates while output gap is positive and unemployment is low.
- Deflation or very low inflation appears while growth weakens.
- Unemployment rises sharply.
- Output gap is highly positive, indicating overheating, or deeply negative, indicating underutilization.

Read-through:
- Rising inflation plus low unemployment and a positive output gap increases tightening risk.
- Falling inflation plus rising unemployment and a negative output gap increases depression or disinflationary slowdown risk.

## Pillar 3: Rates And Yield Curve

Indicators:
- Short rate.
- Long rate.
- Yield curve, usually long rate minus short rate.

Supportive:
- Rates are aligned with growth and inflation.
- Yield curve is positive and credit creation remains healthy.
- Rate cuts or easing are helping without destabilizing the currency.

Neutral:
- Rates are moving but not yet restrictive or stimulative.
- Yield curve is flat but not strongly inverted.

Stressed:
- Short rates rise quickly into high debt burdens or asset bubbles.
- Yield curve flattens or inverts, reducing lending incentives and signaling tightening stress.
- Long rates rise because of inflation, fiscal, currency, or credit risk rather than healthy growth.
- Rates are near zero but growth remains weak, indicating conventional policy is exhausted.

Read-through:
- Rising short rates, a flat/inverted curve, high valuations, and debt stress often point to late cycle or top risk.
- Low rates with weak growth and private credit contraction point to depression or balance-sheet recession.

## Pillar 4: Debt And Fiscal Position

Indicators:
- Net domestic debt as percent of GDP, preferably 12-month moving average.
- Fiscal balance as percent of GDP.
- Debt service where available.

Supportive:
- Debt ratios are stable or falling because incomes grow faster than debt.
- Fiscal balance is stable, improving, or countercyclical without undermining growth.
- Debt service is manageable.

Neutral:
- Debt is rising slowly but income growth, rates, and fiscal capacity remain adequate.
- Fiscal deficits are temporary and well-funded.

Stressed:
- Debt rises faster than GDP or income.
- Fiscal balance deteriorates persistently without restoring growth.
- Debt service rises because rates or debt stocks are high.
- Austerity is imposed into weak growth without offsetting monetary easing.

Read-through:
- Rising debt with strong asset prices and easy credit supports a debt-bubble read.
- Rising debt with weak income, falling assets, and ineffective rates supports depression or deleveraging stress.

## Pillar 5: External, FX, And Reserves

Indicators:
- Real FX versus trade-weighted index.
- Reserve flow as percent of GDP.
- Current account as percent of GDP.
- Currency index.

Supportive:
- Real FX is stable or competitive without disorderly depreciation.
- Reserve flows are stable or positive.
- Current account is sustainable.
- Currency index is stable or rising for healthy reasons.

Neutral:
- FX is adjusting but reserves and current account remain manageable.
- Current account deterioration is cyclical or financed by stable long-term capital.

Stressed:
- Real FX weakens sharply or becomes overvalued and vulnerable.
- Reserve flows turn negative or show capital flight.
- Current account deteriorates materially.
- Currency index falls quickly or policy must defend the currency with high rates.

Read-through:
- External pressure can force tighter policy even when domestic growth is weak.
- Reserve losses plus currency weakness plus current account deterioration are serious emerging-market stress signals.

## Pillar 6: Markets And Valuation

Indicators:
- Currency index, indexed to start.
- Bond total returns, indexed to start.
- Equity total returns, indexed to start.
- P/E ratio on actual earnings.

Supportive:
- Bond and equity returns are positive for fundamental reasons.
- Equity valuations are reasonable relative to earnings and growth.
- Currency performance is consistent with external fundamentals.

Neutral:
- Asset returns are mixed.
- P/E is elevated but partly justified by earnings growth, lower rates, or low risk premia.

Stressed:
- Equity returns surge while P/E expands sharply and earnings do not justify prices.
- Bond returns weaken because rates or credit risk rise.
- Currency losses combine with reserve outflows or inflation pressure.
- Asset returns reverse sharply after a leveraged boom.

Read-through:
- Strong equity returns plus rising P/E, high growth, rising inflation, and tightening risk can indicate a bubble or late-cycle top.
- Falling equities, weak bonds, currency pressure, and debt stress point to financial stress.
- Asset recovery with improving growth and inflation normalization can indicate reflation.

## Cross-Signal Regime Map

### Goldilocks Expansion

Typical pattern:
- Real growth near or above potential.
- Industrial production rising.
- Inflation stable.
- Unemployment falling gradually.
- Output gap near zero.
- Yield curve positive.
- Debt/fiscal/external conditions stable.
- Asset returns positive without extreme valuation.

### Overheating Late Cycle

Typical pattern:
- Real growth above potential.
- Output gap positive.
- Inflation rising.
- Unemployment low.
- Short rates rising.
- Yield curve flattening.
- Debt and asset prices rising.
- Equity returns strong and P/E elevated.

### Bubble Or Top Risk

Typical pattern:
- Asset returns and P/E rise sharply.
- Debt rises faster than GDP.
- Credit conditions are easy or leverage expands.
- Inflation or capacity pressure pushes rates higher.
- Yield curve flattens or inverts.
- Equity or property prices become vulnerable to tightening.

Use `stage-debt-cycle` if the user asks where the country is in the debt cycle.

### Disinflationary Slowdown

Typical pattern:
- Real growth falls below potential.
- Industrial production weakens.
- Inflation decelerates.
- Unemployment rises.
- Output gap turns negative.
- Central bank eases if currency conditions allow.
- Equities weaken; bonds may rally if credit risk is contained.

### Debt Or Financial Stress

Typical pattern:
- Debt burden rises.
- Debt service or rates are restrictive.
- Asset prices fall.
- Yield curve is inverted or policy is constrained.
- Fiscal balance deteriorates.
- Credit creation weakens.

Use `monitor-deleveraging`, `stage-debt-cycle`, or `assess-depression-policy-management` when the user asks specifically about deleveraging or depression management.

### External Pressure

Typical pattern:
- Real FX weakens or becomes vulnerable.
- Currency index falls.
- Reserve flows turn negative.
- Current account deteriorates.
- Rates may need to rise despite weak growth.
- Inflation can rise through import prices.

### Reflation Or Recovery

Typical pattern:
- Growth and industrial production recover.
- Inflation moves away from deflation or stabilizes.
- Unemployment falls.
- Output gap closes.
- Policy remains supportive.
- Debt ratios stabilize as nominal income improves.
- Markets recover with improving fundamentals.

## Scoring

Score each pillar:

```text
supportive = 2
neutral = 1
stressed = 0
unknown = null
```

Interpretation:
- 10-12 points: broadly supportive macro and market conditions, unless one pillar is severely stressed.
- 7-9 points: mixed but generally stable; identify the weakest pillar.
- 4-6 points: vulnerable or transition regime.
- 0-3 points: stressed macro/market conditions.
- Unknown-heavy: lower confidence and list missing data.

Override rules:
- If external/FX/reserves are severely stressed, do not call conditions broadly supportive.
- If debt/fiscal is severely stressed and rates are restrictive, flag financial-stress risk even if growth remains strong.
- If markets/valuation are extremely stretched while rates and inflation rise, flag bubble/top risk even if current growth looks good.
- If growth is weak and rates cannot be cut enough, flag depression/deleveraging risk.

## Suggested Language

Use concise, evidence-backed phrasing:

- "The dashboard points to overheating late cycle: growth is above potential, inflation and the output gap are rising, unemployment is low, rates are moving up, and the curve is flattening."
- "The dashboard points to external pressure: reserve flows have turned negative, the current account is weakening, the currency index is falling, and policy has limited room to ease."
- "The dashboard is mixed: growth and industrial production are strong, but debt, valuation, and yield-curve signals point to late-cycle vulnerability."
