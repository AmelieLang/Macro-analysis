# Ugly Deleverage Rubric

## Definition

An ugly deleveraging occurs when debt burdens are reduced mainly through contraction, defaults, asset-price declines, weak nominal income, unemployment, and fiscal deterioration rather than through a balanced reflation that lifts income faster than debt service and debt stocks.

## Three-Period Template

### 1. Pre-Top/Bubble

Look for:

- Real growth above potential.
- Positive and rising output gap.
- Asset prices rising faster than income or earnings.
- P/E, price-to-income, or price-to-rent ratios at extremes.
- Credit/debt rising faster than income.
- Inflation and labor-market data that look benign or only modestly hot.
- Monetary tightening or currency appreciation into leverage.

Interpretation: the system is vulnerable even if standard macro data look strong.

### 2. Top/Still-Tightening

Look for:

- Asset prices falling before unemployment rises.
- Yield curve flattening or inversion.
- Short rates high relative to falling growth expectations.
- Currency strength, capital pressure, or reserve losses.
- Credit growth slowing or lending standards tightening.
- Fiscal stance still tight because revenues and balances still look good.
- Policymakers relying on lagging inflation, output gap, and unemployment.

Interpretation: this is the highest-risk policy window. Easing that looks "gradual" may be too small relative to asset deflation and debt stocks.

### 3. Free Fall With Easing

Look for:

- Rate cuts alongside continued weak growth.
- Falling inflation or deflation.
- Rising unemployment.
- Falling industrial production.
- Negative output gap.
- Bond rally and lower long yields.
- Equity/property prices still impaired.
- Fiscal balance deteriorating after private demand collapses.
- Banks and borrowers focused on balance-sheet repair rather than new credit.

Interpretation: the economy is no longer responding normally to lower rates. Policy must repair nominal income, collateral, and credit channels.

## Policy Pillars

### Fiscal

Assess:

- General government balance and cyclically adjusted balance.
- Public debt, debt service, and fiscal space.
- Timing and size of stimulus.
- Whether support reaches income, employment, borrowers, and banks.
- Whether austerity or delayed stimulus amplifies private deleveraging.

Ugly signal: fiscal policy stays tight at the top, then turns supportive only after private demand and asset prices have collapsed.

### Monetary

Assess:

- Policy rate, call rate, money-market rates, and real short rates.
- Long rates and yield curve.
- Central bank balance sheet, monetary base, liquidity provision, QE, credit facilities.
- Debt service and credit transmission.
- Bank lending standards and private credit creation.

Ugly signal: nominal rates fall but real rates remain too high for the debt burden, or banks/borrowers do not transmit easing into new spending.

### Currency

Assess:

- Nominal and real effective exchange rates.
- Exchange rate against major funding/trade currencies.
- Reserve flows and intervention.
- Current account.
- External debt, capital flows, and imported inflation/deflation.

Ugly signal: currency appreciation or reserve pressure tightens conditions when domestic balance sheets need reflation.

## Indicator Map

Use these categories:

- Growth: real GDP growth, potential growth, nominal GDP growth, industrial production, capacity utilization.
- Slack: unemployment, output gap, employment, wages.
- Prices: CPI, GDP deflator, inflation expectations where available.
- Debt and credit: private non-financial debt, household debt, corporate debt, government debt, debt service, bank credit, credit standards.
- Rates: policy rate, call rate, long government yield, real rates, yield curve.
- Assets: equity total return, property prices, land prices, P/E, price-to-book, market cap to GDP, credit spreads.
- External/currency: real effective exchange rate, current account, reserve flows, FX intervention.
- Fiscal: fiscal balance, primary balance, cyclically adjusted balance, stimulus measures, bank recapitalization costs.
- Financial system: bank capital, non-performing loans, defaults, bankruptcies, lending standards.

## Leading And Lagging Classification

Usually leading:

- Asset prices.
- Valuation ratios.
- Yield curve.
- Credit spreads and lending standards.
- Debt service, leverage, and credit-to-GDP gap.
- Reserve flows/currency pressure.
- Bank funding stress.
- New issuance, turnover, margin credit, and speculative participation.

Usually lagging:

- Unemployment.
- Headline CPI inflation.
- Fiscal balance.
- Output gap estimates.
- Reported GDP growth.
- Non-performing loans, because recognition can lag the asset-price shock.

Treat industrial production as coincident to early-lagging: it can remain strong near the top but becomes important when it rolls over.

## Scoring

Score each period/pillar as:

- `appropriate`: policy leaning offsets the cycle-phase risk.
- `too tight`: policy amplifies private deleveraging.
- `too modest`: easing exists but is too small or slow for balance-sheet damage.
- `late`: support arrives after the self-reinforcing contraction begins.
- `unknown`: data are missing.

Overall regime:

```text
Ugly deleveraging:
  asset/debt deflation is underway, nominal income is weak, unemployment is rising,
  and policy remains too tight, too modest, too late, or blocked by credit impairment.

Ugly-deleveraging risk:
  leading indicators are breaking while lagging data still look acceptable and policy has not pivoted enough.

Mixed/transition:
  policy is moving toward reflation but transmission and nominal income are not yet clearly repaired.

Not ugly deleveraging:
  nominal income growth, inflation, employment, credit, and fiscal/monetary support are sufficient to reduce debt burdens without contraction.
```

## Policy Mistake Patterns

- Lagging-indicator trap: waiting for unemployment or inflation to confirm stress after asset prices and credit already broke.
- Gradualism trap: cutting rates slowly because recent inflation/output-gap data still look strong.
- Fiscal-balance illusion: treating a top-of-cycle fiscal surplus as durable rather than cyclical.
- Bond-rally misread: interpreting lower long rates as adequate easing when they may signal deflationary demand for safety.
- Bank-solvency neglect: lowering rates without forcing recognition, recapitalization, or credit-channel repair.
- Currency squeeze: allowing real currency appreciation to offset domestic easing.
- Asset-price denial: treating equity/property collapse as a market correction rather than collateral destruction in a leveraged economy.
