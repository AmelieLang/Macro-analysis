# Japan 1984-1994 Case Study

Use this reference as the template for an ugly deflationary deleveraging caused by a late and insufficient policy response after an asset/debt bubble.

## Core Interpretation

Japan entered the early 1990s with asset prices, valuations, debt, and output-gap pressure already stretched. Policymakers tightened into the late bubble, then eased only modestly in 1990-1991 while the leading indicators were already breaking. By the time easing accelerated, debt deflation and damaged balance sheets had taken over: equity prices had collapsed, real growth fell below potential, industrial production rolled over, unemployment rose with a lag, and fiscal support arrived after private-sector weakness was already self-reinforcing.

## Period 1: 1984-1990 - Bubble Build-Up And Tightening

### Economic Indicators

- Real growth ran above declining potential growth for much of the late 1980s, especially around 1988-1989.
- Inflation was low in the mid-1980s, then rose toward roughly 3-4 percent around 1990-1991.
- Industrial production rose strongly from 1987 into 1991, a late-cycle strength signal that could mask balance-sheet fragility.
- Unemployment fell from around 3 percent in 1987 toward roughly 2 percent by 1990-1991, making labor markets look healthy.
- Output gap moved from negative in 1986-1987 to strongly positive by 1990-1991.
- Net domestic debt rose sharply from a little above 200 percent of GDP toward the mid-240 percent area by 1990.
- Equity total returns surged through 1989-1990, then peaked.
- P/E ratios rose from the low 30s in the mid-1980s to around 70-80 near the top.
- Currency appreciated materially after 1985, then retraced into 1990.

### Policy Pillars

- Fiscal: fiscal balance improved into a large surplus by 1990-1991. This looked prudent but left fiscal policy restrictive at the top.
- Monetary: short rates rose into 1990, with the short rate reaching around 8 percent. Long rates rose less, creating a flatter/inverted curve.
- Currency: yen appreciation after 1985 tightened financial conditions through the external channel, then the currency weakened into 1990.

### Lesson

The economy looked strong on lagging data: unemployment was low, output gap was positive, industrial production was high, and inflation was rising. The dangerous signals were valuations, debt, asset-price excess, and the yield curve/rate shock.

## Period 2: 1990-1991 - Top, Still Tightening, And Modest Easing

### Economic Indicators

- Equity prices broke first. The equity total return chart peaks around 1989-1990 and falls sharply before the real economy fully deteriorates.
- P/E ratios were still extreme around 1990-1991, then began to compress violently as prices and earnings expectations adjusted.
- Short rates stayed high around the top and only began to fall gradually.
- The yield curve was flat to inverted around 1990-1991, signaling tight money and poor lending incentives.
- Output gap and unemployment still looked benign for a while, which created a lagging-indicator trap.
- Industrial production was near its high around 1991, then started to fall.
- Reserve flows turned sharply negative around 1989-1990.
- Current account fell toward roughly 1-2 percent of GDP around 1990.
- Fiscal balance stayed strongly positive, so fiscal policy did not offset the private-sector balance-sheet shock.

### Policy Pillars

- Fiscal: still tight or insufficiently supportive. The surplus delayed reflation and balance-sheet support.
- Monetary: easing was too modest relative to the asset-price collapse and debt burden. Nominal rates fell, but not fast enough to prevent real rates and debt-service stress from biting.
- Currency: currency movements did not provide a sustained reflationary offset. External and reserve-flow pressure complicated the response.

### Lesson

The key wrong decision was not recognizing that the collapse in asset prices and credit-sensitive indicators had made the old inflation/output-gap framework stale. Policymakers reacted to lagging strength, not the leading deflationary shock.

## Period 3: After 1991 - Free Fall Even With Easing

### Economic Indicators

- Real growth fell below potential after 1991 and approached zero by 1993-1994.
- Inflation slowed sharply after 1991, moving toward low inflation/deflationary pressure.
- Industrial production fell from its peak into 1994.
- Unemployment rose only after the turn, moving from roughly 2 percent in 1991-1992 toward nearly 3 percent by 1994.
- Output gap fell from a large positive level into negative territory by 1993-1994.
- Short rates fell materially, but the economy did not respond normally because balance sheets and credit transmission were impaired.
- Long rates also fell and bond total returns rose strongly, classic depression/deleveraging price action.
- Net domestic debt stayed very high, with debt burdens not resolved by rate cuts alone.
- Currency appreciated again after 1992-1993, adding deflationary pressure.
- Fiscal balance deteriorated only after the downturn was underway, moving from large surplus toward deficit by 1994.
- Equity total returns continued to fall, and P/E ratios compressed from bubble extremes.

### Policy Pillars

- Fiscal: stimulus and deficits arrived late. The fiscal balance finally fell, but after the private-sector contraction had become self-reinforcing.
- Monetary: rate cuts became larger but were no longer enough. Lower rates helped bonds but did not restore private credit creation or asset collateral values.
- Currency: renewed real currency appreciation tightened conditions and made reflation harder.

### Lesson

Once asset deflation, collateral impairment, weak credit, and falling nominal income reinforce each other, ordinary easing can become ineffective. The policy response must be earlier, larger, and balance-sheet-focused.

## Leading Versus Lagging Indicators In This Case

Leading or coincident warning indicators:

- Equity prices and equity total returns.
- P/E ratio and valuation excess.
- Yield curve flattening/inversion.
- Short-rate shock and real-rate pressure.
- Credit/debt burden and debt service.
- Reserve flows and external pressure.
- Industrial production rollover after the peak.
- Currency appreciation when the economy needs reflation.
- Asset-price collateral decline.

Lagging indicators that can fool policymakers:

- Unemployment rate.
- Headline inflation.
- Output gap estimates.
- Real GDP growth, especially when released with lags or revisions.
- Fiscal balance, which may look strong at the top and deteriorate only after automatic stabilizers operate.
- Industrial production while it is still near peak levels.

## Ugly-Deleveraging Causal Chain

```text
Bubble valuations and debt rise
-> policy tightens into the top
-> asset prices fall and collateral weakens
-> debt burdens remain high while income expectations fall
-> lenders and borrowers pull back
-> spending falls, which reduces someone else's income
-> output and inflation fall with a lag
-> unemployment rises with a longer lag
-> fiscal balance deteriorates after private demand is already weak
-> rate cuts help bonds but do not quickly repair balance sheets
-> deleveraging becomes ugly unless policy reflates nominal income and repairs credit channels
```

## Policy Counterfactual Template

A better response would usually include:

- Faster monetary easing once asset prices and credit-sensitive indicators break.
- Explicit focus on real rates and nominal income growth, not only headline inflation.
- Liquidity and solvency support for systemically important financial institutions.
- Fiscal support earlier in the free-fall phase, with measures targeted at income and balance-sheet repair.
- Avoiding pro-cyclical currency appreciation where possible.
- Macroprudential tightening during the bubble rather than relying on broad tightening after leverage is already embedded.
