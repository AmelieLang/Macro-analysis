# Inflationary Deleveraging In Non-Reserve Currency Economies

Use this reference when applying the ugly-deleveraging model to non-reserve-currency economies, balance-of-payments crises, foreign-currency debt cycles, and the Korea 1990s/Asian Financial Crisis template.

## Core Model

Inflationary deleveragings follow a debt-cycle sequence that resembles deflationary deleveragings in the early, bubble, and top stages, but the depression mechanics are different. The critical difference is the currency constraint:

- Capital inflows inflate the boom.
- Debt is often owed in currencies the domestic central bank cannot print.
- Currency depreciation raises local-currency debt burdens.
- Defending the currency with reserves or higher rates can deepen the domestic contraction.
- Letting the currency go can create import-price inflation and a debt-service shock.

The central policy problem is therefore not just "ease enough to offset deflation." It is "ease enough to prevent depression while preserving enough currency confidence to avoid an inflationary spiral."

## Indicators To Use

### Capital Flow And External Financing Indicators

- Capital inflows and outflows: portfolio flows, bank lending, FDI, rollover rates, and resident outflows.
- Current account balance and trade balance.
- Foreign-exchange reserves and reserve drawdown pace.
- FX reserves relative to short-term external debt, imports, broad money, and monthly capital outflows.
- External debt, especially short-term external debt.
- Foreign-currency debt share in banks, corporates, and government.
- External debt service relative to exports and reserves.
- Real and nominal effective exchange rates.

### Domestic Bubble Indicators

- Real GDP growth versus potential growth.
- Industrial production and export growth.
- Inflation and real policy rates.
- Domestic credit growth, corporate leverage, bank balance-sheet growth.
- Asset prices: equity total returns, property prices, and land prices.
- Equity valuation: P/E, market cap to GDP, turnover, and issuance.
- Investment share of GDP, capacity expansion, inventories, construction, and overinvestment signals.

### Policy Indicators

- Monetary: policy rate, money-market rate, real rate, yield curve, liquidity support, lender-of-last-resort actions.
- Currency: reserves sold, rate hikes to defend the currency, FX intervention, capital controls, IMF support, debt rollover deals.
- Fiscal: fiscal balance, guarantees, bank recapitalization, corporate restructuring support, austerity, and stimulus.
- Macroprudential and supervisory: FX borrowing limits, maturity mismatch controls, bank capital, connected lending, chaebol leverage, and disclosure.

### Lagging Indicators

- Unemployment.
- Headline inflation before the currency break.
- Fiscal balance, which may look healthy before recession.
- Reported non-performing loans, because recognition lags asset-price and exchange-rate shocks.
- GDP growth during the late bubble, which can remain high because foreign funding is still financing demand.

## Stage 1: Early Cycle

### Generic Pattern

In the healthy upswing, capital inflows are supported by fundamentals. The country is competitive, debt levels are manageable, balance sheets are healthy, and borrowed funds finance productive investment. Export income and domestic income rise enough to service new debt.

Currency demand improves because foreigners want to invest and because exports are competitive. The central bank can allow appreciation, cut rates, or accumulate reserves. If it buys foreign currency to prevent excessive appreciation, reserves rise and domestic liquidity increases, which can further support growth.

### Korea Template: Early 1990s

Korea in the early 1990s fits the healthy-upswing part of the cycle:

- Real growth was strong, often around the high-single-digit range.
- Industrial production and exports were robust.
- Financial liberalization and global integration made Korea more attractive to foreign capital.
- Inflation moderated compared with earlier decades.
- Foreign-exchange reserves rose, giving policymakers more room.
- Borrowing still appeared productive because income and export capacity were growing.

### Logic Of Evolution

The early cycle is self-reinforcing:

```text
Competitive currency and strong fundamentals
-> foreign capital enters
-> productive investment rises
-> exports, income, and asset prices improve
-> reserves accumulate or rates can fall
-> confidence improves
-> more capital enters
```

The warning is that the same flows that finance productive investment can later finance overinvestment and leverage once the best opportunities have been saturated.

## Stage 2: Debt Bubble

### Generic Pattern

The bubble begins when strong capital inflows, good asset returns, and strong growth pull in more money than the economy can productively absorb. More capital chases fewer high-return investments. Debt begins rising faster than income, and the country becomes more dependent on foreign financing.

The currency and domestic assets may become expensive. Exports become less competitive, imports become cheaper, and the current account deteriorates. The economy can still look strong because foreign borrowing is financing investment, consumption, and asset purchases.

### Korea Template: 1994-1997

Korea's bubble phase emerged between roughly 1994 and 1997:

- Capital inflows surged after liberalization.
- Banks accumulated large short-term foreign liabilities.
- Domestic lending expanded into corporates and industrial capacity.
- Chaebol leverage and overinvestment rose.
- Maturity mismatch grew: banks borrowed short-term abroad and funded longer-term domestic assets.
- Current account deficits widened materially.
- External debt, especially short-term debt, became the key fragility.
- Equity returns and valuation measures were strong even as external vulnerability increased.
- Supervisory capacity lagged financial liberalization.

### Indicators Carrying The Diagnosis

The most important indicators are not just high growth and asset prices. The bubble diagnosis depends on the combination:

```text
Strong growth + rising asset prices
+ current account deterioration
+ short-term foreign-currency debt
+ reserve coverage weakening
+ corporate/bank leverage
+ maturity mismatch
= non-reserve-currency bubble vulnerability
```

### Logic Of Evolution

The bubble evolves because each good outcome justifies more borrowing:

```text
Capital inflows produce high returns
-> high returns attract more inflows
-> banks and corporates borrow more foreign currency
-> investment and asset prices rise
-> current account deteriorates and currency competitiveness fades
-> the economy depends on rollover of foreign funding
-> a small shock can stop the flow
```

## Stage 3: Top And Currency Defense

### Generic Pattern

The top begins when the capital inflow pace slows or reverses. The trigger can be domestic weakness, a current-account problem, a policy tightening in the funding currency, a commodity shock, political risk, or contagion from another country.

Once flows reverse, the cycle flips:

```text
Capital inflows slow
-> asset prices and currency fall
-> debt burdens in foreign currency rise
-> borrowers look less creditworthy
-> lenders refuse rollover
-> reserves fall or rates rise
-> growth weakens
-> more capital exits
```

Policymakers usually try to defend the currency through reserves, rate hikes, public confidence statements, guarantees, or capital controls. These defenses rarely solve the underlying mismatch when the required interest rate is too painful for the domestic economy and reserves are finite.

### Korea Template: 1997

Korea's top and currency-defense phase was triggered by regional contagion after the Thai baht devaluation in July 1997. Investors reassessed Asian credit risk and focused on Korea's short-term foreign debt, banking weakness, and corporate leverage.

Key indicators:

- Foreign creditors reduced exposure and refused rollover.
- The won came under severe pressure.
- FX reserves were spent to defend the currency.
- Short-term external debt was large relative to usable reserves.
- Domestic interest rates were raised sharply.
- Equity prices fell and credit conditions tightened.
- The current account deficit and bank/corporate FX liabilities made the economy dependent on foreign confidence.

### Policy Pillars

Fiscal:

- The initial problem was not a classic fiscal crisis; it was an external funding and private-sector balance-sheet crisis.
- Fiscal capacity mattered later for bank recapitalization, guarantees, and restructuring.

Monetary:

- Higher rates were used to defend the won and restore creditor confidence.
- This defense worsened pressure on highly leveraged corporates and banks.
- Lower rates would have helped domestic borrowers but risked faster capital flight before external financing was secured.

Currency:

- Reserve sales bought time but could not cover the full rollover problem.
- The won needed to adjust, but disorderly depreciation worsened FX debt burdens.
- The key solvency issue was not only the exchange rate level; it was the interaction between depreciation, short-term FX debt, and reserve exhaustion.

### Logic Of Evolution

Korea illustrates why the currency-defense phase is brief and unstable:

```text
Foreign creditors question rollover
-> central bank sells reserves and raises rates
-> reserves fall, confirming vulnerability
-> high rates hurt domestic balance sheets
-> asset prices and confidence weaken
-> more creditors demand exit
-> defense becomes self-defeating
```

## Stage 4: Letting The Currency Go And Inflationary Depression

### Generic Pattern

When reserves and rate hikes cannot stabilize the currency, policymakers eventually let the currency fall, seek external support, restructure debt, impose controls, or combine several of these.

The depression is inflationary when depreciation and FX debt dominate:

- Import prices rise.
- Local-currency value of foreign debt jumps.
- Real incomes fall.
- Banks and corporates face solvency pressure.
- Defaults and unemployment rise.
- Policy must defend currency credibility while supporting growth and the financial system.

This differs from deflationary deleveraging, where the main issue is that policy cannot create enough nominal income and credit expansion. In inflationary deleveraging, policy may be able to print local currency, but printing can worsen currency confidence.

### Korea Template: Late 1997-1998

Korea moved from defense to external support and restructuring:

- The won depreciated sharply.
- Korea entered an IMF-supported program in December 1997.
- Foreign bank creditors were pushed into rollover/rescheduling arrangements.
- Domestic rates were raised sharply early in the program to stabilize the exchange rate.
- Banks and corporates underwent restructuring.
- Output contracted sharply in 1998.
- Unemployment rose with a lag.
- The current account swung from deficit toward large surplus as imports collapsed and exports became more competitive after depreciation.
- Inflation rose temporarily because of depreciation but did not become a sustained hyperinflationary spiral because policy credibility, external support, restructuring, and export recovery stabilized the system.

### Policy Pillars

Fiscal:

- The government had to support financial-sector restructuring and social costs after the shock.
- Fiscal balance can worsen after the private-sector crisis, even if it was not the original cause.

Monetary:

- High rates were used initially to stop currency free fall.
- Once rollover, external support, and currency stabilization improved, policy could ease.
- The sequence matters: defend enough to stop the run, then ease and restructure enough to avoid prolonged depression.

Currency:

- Depreciation restored some competitiveness but also raised FX debt burdens.
- External liquidity support and debt rescheduling were essential because the domestic central bank could not print dollars.
- The key repair was restoring rollover confidence and rebuilding reserves.

### Logic Of Evolution

```text
Currency defense fails
-> depreciation raises FX debt burden
-> IMF/external support supplies hard currency
-> rollover/rescheduling reduces immediate funding gap
-> restructuring recognizes losses and repairs banks/corporates
-> current account improves through import compression and competitiveness
-> reserves recover and rates can fall
-> growth rebounds if confidence returns
```

## Stage 5: Recovery And Post-Crisis Repair

### Generic Pattern

Recovery from inflationary deleveraging requires external and domestic balance-sheet repair:

- Current account moves into surplus.
- Reserves rebuild.
- Short-term external debt falls relative to reserves.
- Banks are recapitalized or resolved.
- Corporate leverage falls.
- FX mismatches are reduced.
- Policy credibility returns.
- Growth resumes without dependence on unstable foreign borrowing.

### Korea Template: 1999 And After

Korea recovered relatively quickly compared with many inflationary crises because:

- The current account adjusted sharply.
- External debt maturities were restructured.
- Reserves rebuilt.
- The exchange-rate adjustment restored competitiveness.
- Corporate and financial reforms reduced some vulnerabilities.
- Korea retained export capacity and institutional credibility.

This is why Korea is a classic inflationary debt-cycle case but not a hyperinflationary collapse. The currency shock was severe, yet the repair mechanisms worked before the spiral became unbounded.

## Full Korea Timeline Model

```text
Early 1990s: healthy capital-inflow upswing
  high growth, export strength, improving reserves, productive investment

1994-1997: debt bubble
  financial liberalization, short-term FX borrowing, corporate leverage, current-account deficit, strong assets

July-November 1997: top and currency defense
  Thai baht contagion, rollover fears, reserve drain, rate hikes, won pressure, equity weakness

December 1997-1998: currency break and inflationary depression
  IMF program, sharp depreciation, high rates, debt rollover, output contraction, unemployment rise

1999 onward: repair
  current-account surplus, reserve rebuild, restructuring, competitiveness recovery, growth rebound
```

## Leading Versus Lagging Indicators In Korea

Leading indicators:

- Short-term external debt/reserves.
- Reserve drawdown.
- Rollover rates and foreign bank exposure.
- Current account deterioration.
- Real effective exchange-rate overvaluation.
- Corporate leverage and interest coverage.
- Bank FX maturity mismatch.
- Equity prices and P/E.
- Capital-flow reversal and contagion from peer countries.

Lagging indicators:

- Unemployment.
- Real GDP after foreign funding suddenly stops.
- Headline inflation before depreciation.
- Reported non-performing loans.
- Fiscal balance.
- Official confidence statements and credit ratings when they adjust late.

## What Makes Korea A Non-Reserve-Currency Case

Korea did not owe primarily in a currency it could print. That changed the policy tradeoff:

- Cutting rates too soon risked faster capital flight.
- Raising rates defended the currency but hurt domestic borrowers.
- Selling reserves delayed depreciation but reduced the buffer.
- Depreciation restored competitiveness but increased local-currency FX debt burdens.
- External hard-currency support and rollover agreements were needed because domestic money creation could not repay dollar debts.

The core lesson is that non-reserve-currency deleveragings are governed by the availability and confidence of external funding. The key dashboard is therefore external-financing fragility plus domestic balance-sheet leverage.

## Beautiful Versus Ugly Inflationary Deleveraging Management

Use this section when the user asks whether a country managed, is managing, or is trending toward managing an inflationary deleveraging well. In this reference, `beautiful` means policymakers are closing the external financing gap, stabilizing the currency, repairing balance sheets, and smoothing the downturn without creating a renewed currency/inflation spiral. `Ugly` means policy is delaying adjustment, losing currency credibility, worsening capital flight, or allowing disorderly defaults to feed back into the balance-of-payments crisis.

Assess four policy pillars:

```text
1. Managing the currency
2. Closing external imbalances
3. Smoothing the downturn
4. Managing bad debts/defaults
```

Score each pillar as `well-managed`, `mixed`, `poorly-managed`, or `unknown`. Use official data first, then official program documents and central-bank/finance-ministry communications.

### 1. Managing The Currency

Well-managed pattern:

- Policymakers do not pretend forever that devaluation is impossible when the balance of payments has already broken.
- If devaluation is needed, it is large enough to create a two-way market: investors no longer have a one-way expectation of further currency weakness.
- Currency adjustment is paired with credible external financing, rate policy, and restructuring, so depreciation does not become a self-feeding inflation spiral.
- Reserves are used to smooth disorderly moves, not to defend an unsustainable level until reserves are exhausted.

Poorly-managed pattern:

- Policymakers are widely expected to allow currency weakness, creating more downward pressure on the currency and higher interest rates.
- The initial devaluation is too small, so further devaluations are expected.
- Markets price a one-way depreciation trade, pushing up interest rates, inflation expectations, and capital flight.
- Public commitments to defend the currency lack reserve adequacy, rollover support, or credible policy backing.

Official indicators:

- Spot exchange rate, nominal effective exchange rate, real effective exchange rate.
- Forward exchange rate, NDFs, forward premium/discount, cross-currency basis where available.
- FX reserves, usable reserves, reserve drawdown pace, intervention data.
- Short-term external debt/reserves.
- Policy rate, money-market rate, real rate, and yield curve.
- Inflation expectations, CPI pass-through, import prices.
- Official statements, IMF program documents, central-bank intervention disclosures.

### 2. Closing External Imbalances

Well-managed pattern:

- Tight enough monetary policy and currency adjustment reduce domestic demand in line with the fall in national income.
- Policy creates incentives for investors to stay in the currency, including interest rates high enough to compensate for expected depreciation and inflation risk.
- Current account improves through import compression and export competitiveness rather than only through collapse.
- External financing needs shrink relative to reserves and official support.

Poorly-managed pattern:

- Policymakers favor short-term domestic conditions too much, leaving monetary policy too loose for the currency problem.
- Loose policy delays domestic adjustment, stokes inflation, and worsens capital flight.
- Policymakers try to stop capital outflow mainly with capital controls or restrictive measures while the underlying return/currency problem remains unresolved.
- Current-account improvement is delayed or offset by capital flight, reserve loss, and falling confidence.

Official indicators:

- Current account and trade balance as percent of GDP.
- Import volumes/value, export volumes/value, terms of trade.
- Capital account/financial account flows, portfolio flows, bank flows, resident outflows.
- Reserves/imports, reserves/short-term external debt, reserve drawdown.
- Policy rate minus inflation and expected depreciation.
- Capital-control measures, macroprudential FX measures, and official financing gaps.

### 3. Smoothing The Downturn

Well-managed pattern:

- Reserves are used judiciously to smooth the withdrawal of foreign capital while the country works to close external imbalances.
- Fiscal and monetary support are sequenced with currency stabilization: enough support to prevent unnecessary collapse, but not so much that it restarts capital flight.
- External support, swap lines, IMF financing, and rollover agreements buy time for adjustment.
- Social and banking support are targeted at preserving essential income and credit channels.

Poorly-managed pattern:

- Policymakers rely on reserve sales to maintain unsustainably high spending and delay adjustment.
- Reserves are depleted without reducing the external financing gap.
- Stimulus is too broad or too early relative to currency stabilization, feeding inflation and outflows.
- Austerity is too blunt, causing avoidable output collapse and weakening debt repayment capacity.

Official indicators:

- Reserve loss relative to imports, short-term debt, and capital outflows.
- Fiscal balance, primary balance, cyclically adjusted balance, and crisis spending.
- IMF/multilateral disbursements, swap lines, official loans, and creditor rollover volumes.
- Real GDP, industrial production, unemployment, wages, poverty/social-support data where official.
- Bank lending, deposits, liquidity facilities, and credit growth.

### 4. Managing Bad Debts And Defaults

Well-managed pattern:

- Policymakers work through debts of entities that are over-indebted.
- Gaps are filled with credible capital, restructuring, rollover agreements, bank recapitalization, guarantees, or orderly resolution.
- Losses are recognized enough to restore confidence without forcing unnecessary disorderly liquidation.
- Systemically important institutions are stabilized while nonviable entities are restructured or resolved.

Poorly-managed pattern:

- Policymakers allow disorderly defaults that increase uncertainty and capital flight.
- Insolvent banks or corporates are kept alive without credible restructuring, tying up credit and damaging confidence.
- Debt standstills, controls, or guarantees are announced without clear rules, making investors rush for the exit.
- FX-debt losses are ignored until depreciation has already made them systemic.

Official indicators:

- Non-performing loans, bankruptcies, default rates, arrears, and corporate interest coverage.
- Bank capital, provisioning, liquidity, deposit flows, and recapitalization amounts.
- External debt rollover/rescheduling, maturity extensions, creditor agreements.
- Government guarantees, bank-resolution actions, fiscal cost of restructuring.
- Corporate leverage, FX debt exposure, debt service/export income, debt service/cash flow.

### Policy Quality Scoring

Use this interpretation:

```text
4 well-managed pillars: beautiful inflationary deleveraging; policy is likely doing well.
3 well-managed, 1 mixed/unknown: mostly beautiful; monitor the weak pillar.
2 well-managed and 2 mixed/poor: mixed/transition; direction matters.
1 or fewer well-managed, or 2+ poorly-managed pillars: ugly inflationary deleveraging.
Unknown-heavy: insufficient data; identify missing official policy and market indicators.
```

When judging whether a country is "trending better," focus on direction:

- Currency expectations move from one-way depreciation to two-way trading.
- Current account improves and external financing gap narrows.
- Capital outflow slows and official financing/rollover support stabilizes hard-currency liquidity.
- Short rates can fall without renewed currency collapse.
- Inflation expectations and realized inflation begin to fall after currency stabilization.
- Bank/corporate restructuring reduces uncertainty and rollover risk.

## Bottom-Reached Checklist

Use this section when the user asks whether a country that has already gone through an inflationary deleveraging has reached bottom. The bottom is not simply the lowest GDP print or the cheapest currency level. It is the point where the external-financing spiral stops worsening and the currency/rates/inflation feedback loop begins to stabilize.

### Typical Bottom Signals

Score each signal as `yes`, `mixed`, `no`, or `unknown`.

1. Current account improves sharply after import collapse.
   - Look for imports falling enough to swing the current account toward balance or surplus.
   - In the archetype, the current account improves by roughly 8 percent of GDP.
   - This is often driven by import compression first, then later by currency-led export competitiveness.

2. Capital inflows stop declining and stabilize.
   - Track portfolio flows, bank rollover, FDI, external issuance, and official financing.
   - The bottom is closer when the pace of capital-flow deterioration stops, even before flows become strongly positive.

3. Capital flight abates.
   - Watch resident outflows, deposit dollarization, reserve losses, parallel-market pressure, and forward-market stress.
   - The key signal is that domestic money stops trying to leave at an accelerating pace.

4. External official support appears when reserves are limited.
   - IMF programs, regional facilities, swap lines, creditor rollover agreements, or multilateral financing can provide a stable source of hard currency.
   - Support matters most when usable reserves cannot cover short-term external debt and near-term capital outflow.

5. Short-term interest rates begin to fall, while long-term rates stay elevated.
   - In the archetype, short rates often begin to come down about a year after the bottom.
   - After peaking, short rates often return toward pre-crisis levels over roughly two years.
   - Falling short rates are stimulative, but elevated long rates can still reflect inflation risk, default risk, and weak credibility.

6. Forward currency price rallies relative to spot as short rates come down.
   - This means forward-market stress is easing and expected depreciation pressure is moderating.
   - Use forwards, cross-currency basis, NDFs, or implied forward points where available.

7. Currency stabilization brings inflation down.
   - Inflation usually falls after the currency stops collapsing and import-price pass-through fades.
   - In the archetype, inflation often takes nearly two years after the bottom to return to pre-crisis levels.

### Bottom Assessment Logic

Use this interpretation:

```text
6-7 yes: bottom likely reached; recovery/repair phase is probably underway.
4-5 yes: bottom forming; still vulnerable to relapse if reserves, rollover, or inflation credibility weaken.
2-3 yes: stabilization attempt, not a confirmed bottom.
0-1 yes: still in the falling phase or currency-defense/currency-break phase.
unknown-heavy: insufficient data; identify the missing external-financing and currency-market indicators.
```

The strongest bottom evidence is a combined pattern:

```text
current-account adjustment
+ stabilizing capital flows
+ slower capital flight/reserve loss
+ external hard-currency backstop
+ falling short rates without renewed currency collapse
+ forward currency improvement
+ inflation deceleration
= external-financing spiral has likely stopped worsening
```

### Korea Bottom Template

For Korea, the bottoming process after the 1997 crisis fits this sequence:

- The current account moved from deficit toward large surplus as imports collapsed and the cheaper won supported exports.
- External support and creditor rollover/rescheduling helped stop the hard-currency funding run.
- Reserve pressure eased after the IMF program and rollover arrangements.
- High short rates used in the defense phase could later fall as the currency stabilized.
- Inflation rose after depreciation but did not spiral because external support, restructuring, and policy credibility stabilized the won.
- Growth recovered once the external constraint eased and domestic financial restructuring progressed.

The lesson is that the bottom was created by external adjustment plus financing repair, not by lower rates alone.

## Practical Policy Lessons

- Monitor capital flows before they show up in GDP.
- Treat short-term FX debt/reserves as a core crisis indicator.
- Regulate maturity mismatch and unhedged FX borrowing during the boom, not after the top.
- Do not rely on reserves alone when rollover needs exceed usable reserves.
- Recognize that currency defense through rate hikes can be necessary but contractionary.
- Pair external liquidity with debt rollover/restructuring; liquidity without maturity repair may only delay the run.
- Letting the currency adjust can restore competitiveness, but only if FX debt burdens and bank solvency are addressed.
- Non-reserve-currency economies need a different easing rule than reserve-currency economies because domestic money creation cannot extinguish foreign-currency liabilities.
