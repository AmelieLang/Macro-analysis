# Policy Management Rubric For Debt-Cycle Depressions

## Core Question

Judge whether policymakers are managing a debt-cycle downturn well enough to avoid or exit an ugly depression. Score the policy mix across four moments:

1. Bubble prevention and management.
2. Top management.
3. Depression response.
4. Beautiful-deleveraging transition.

The goal is not maximum easing at all times. Good management balances credit restraint before the bust, fast liquidity support during the bust, sustained fiscal support when private credit contracts, protection of systemic institutions, and enough reflation to bring nominal growth above nominal rates without destroying currency confidence.

## Evidence Checklist

Collect phase-appropriate evidence:

- Policy rates, yield curve, real rates, central bank balance sheet, asset purchases, liquidity facilities, reserve money, and lender-of-last-resort actions.
- Fiscal balance, stimulus, transfers, automatic stabilizers, austerity, guarantees, recapitalizations, and debt sustainability.
- Macroprudential tools, loan-to-value rules, capital requirements, reserve requirements, sector credit limits, underwriting standards, and countercyclical buffers.
- Bank and non-bank interventions, deposit guarantees, resolution actions, recapitalizations, bail-ins, nationalizations, and orderly failures.
- Credit growth, asset prices, leverage, debt service, defaults, unemployment, inflation, nominal growth, currency pressure, and social stress.

Use evidence available at the time when judging whether policymakers acted well.

## Bubble Phase

Well managed:
- Central banks and regulators consider debt growth and its effect on asset markets, not just inflation and current real growth.
- Policymakers prevent or restrain bubbles before they become systemically dangerous.
- Macroprudential policies target restraints where bubbles are emerging while allowing healthy credit growth elsewhere.
- Fiscal policy tightens or avoids procyclical stimulus when private credit and asset markets are overheating.
- Authorities raise underwriting quality, reduce leverage incentives, and monitor shadow banking or offshore credit channels.

Poorly managed:
- Policymakers focus only on inflation and/or near-term growth while ignoring debt bubbles in investment assets.
- Credit remains cheap for too long relative to leverage, asset prices, and repayment capacity.
- Speculators and lenders extrapolate past success and expand debt-financed investments without policy restraint.
- Macroprudential controls are absent, too broad, too late, or aimed at the wrong sectors.
- Fiscal policy amplifies the boom through procyclical deficits, subsidies, guarantees, or tax incentives.

Bubble score:
- `well managed`: policy actively restrains dangerous leverage while preserving productive credit.
- `mixed`: some restraint exists but major asset/debt excess continues.
- `poorly managed`: policy ignores or fuels the bubble.
- `unknown`: evidence on credit policy, fiscal stance, or asset leverage is unavailable.

## Top Phase

Well managed:
- Central banks and regulators constrict the bubble with broad monetary policy or well-chosen macroprudential policies.
- Once the bubble breaks, policymakers ease selectively and quickly enough to avoid unnecessary systemic damage.
- Tightening is calibrated to the most leveraged and rate-sensitive sectors, not just economy-wide averages.
- Authorities distinguish between cooling speculation and triggering a disorderly credit contraction.

Poorly managed:
- Central banks continue tightening well after the bubble has burst.
- Policymakers keep focusing on lagging inflation or employment strength while credit stress is already spreading.
- Liquidity support is delayed because stress first appears in frothy or stigmatized sectors.
- Rate hikes, regulatory tightening, or fiscal tightening intensify forced selling and credit withdrawal.

Top score:
- `well managed`: tightens enough to pop or cool excess, then pivots before the bust becomes self-reinforcing.
- `mixed`: recognizes the turn but responds late or unevenly.
- `poorly managed`: keeps tightening into the break or ignores spreading credit stress.
- `unknown`: timing of tightening/easing and credit stress is unclear.

## Depression Phase

Well managed:
- Central banks provide ample liquidity quickly.
- Short rates are cut rapidly until they reach the effective lower bound.
- Policymakers then pursue aggressive monetization or equivalent balance-sheet expansion when conventional easing is exhausted.
- Macroprudential easing is targeted to restart high-quality credit growth and protect systemically important entities.
- Governments pursue aggressive and sustained fiscal stimulus, easing past the turn rather than withdrawing too early.
- Systemically important institutions are protected, recapitalized, guaranteed, merged, or resolved in ways that prevent collapse of the payments and credit system.

Poorly managed:
- Central banks are slow to cut rates.
- Liquidity support is too limited, too stigmatized, or too narrow.
- Policymakers tighten too early after initial stabilization.
- Authorities wait too long to pursue aggressive monetization or balance-sheet expansion.
- Governments pursue austerity without adequate monetary easing or private-sector recovery.
- Systemically important institutions are left damaged, undercapitalized, or failed in disorderly ways.
- Policy allows deflationary defaults and restructurings to overwhelm income growth and credit creation.

Depression score:
- `well managed`: fast liquidity, rapid rate cuts, aggressive reflation after the lower bound, sustained fiscal support, and systemic stabilization.
- `mixed`: important support occurs but is late, undersized, poorly targeted, or withdrawn too early.
- `poorly managed`: policy allows a self-reinforcing credit contraction and systemic failures.
- `unknown`: crisis facilities, fiscal stance, or banking response is unclear.

## Beautiful-Deleveraging Phase

Well managed:
- Reflation begins with aggressive monetization through asset purchases, currency depreciation, or other measures large enough to bring nominal growth above nominal rates.
- Stimulative macroprudential policies protect systemically important entities and stimulate high-quality credit growth.
- Non-systemically important institutions are allowed to fail in an orderly way.
- Policymakers balance the depressive forces of defaults and austerity with reflationary forces of debt monetization, currency declines, and fiscal stimulus.
- The policy mix supports income growth, employment, and credit repair while avoiding runaway inflation or currency collapse.

Poorly managed:
- Initial monetization stutters and starts.
- Asset purchases are too muted or concentrated in cash-like instruments, so they do not create enough wealth effect or risk appetite.
- Central bank stimulus is undermined by fiscal austerity.
- Overindebted entities are protected even though they are not systemically important, creating zombie banks, zombie firms, and malinvestment.
- Policymakers print too much money or allow faith in the currency to collapse, producing an ugly inflationary depression.
- Reflation helps asset prices but fails to restore income growth above debt growth.

Beautiful-deleveraging score:
- `well managed`: nominal growth rises above nominal rates, systemic credit channels stabilize, non-systemic failures are orderly, and inflation/currency risks remain controlled.
- `mixed`: reflation is working but uneven, delayed, or partly offset by austerity or zombie support.
- `poorly managed`: policy remains deflationary, protects zombies, or flips into uncontrolled inflation/currency collapse.
- `unknown`: nominal growth, asset purchases, fiscal stance, or institutional resolution evidence is missing.

## Overall Score

Use this guide:

```text
Well managed:
  Most relevant phase scores are well managed; the policy mix is timely, balanced, targeted, and moves nominal growth above debt burdens without systemic collapse.

Mixed:
  Policy contains important correct actions and important mistakes; outcomes depend on whether reflation strengthens or errors persist.

Poorly managed:
  Policy ignores bubbles, tightens after the turn, delays liquidity/monetization, withdraws fiscal support too early, allows systemic institutions to fail disorderly, protects zombies, or destroys currency confidence.

Insufficient data:
  Key policy actions or macro outcomes cannot be verified.
```

## Risk Path Classification

After scoring policy management, classify the likely path:

- `Beautiful deleveraging`: timely liquidity, sustained fiscal support, controlled monetization, protected systemic institutions, orderly non-systemic failures, and nominal growth above nominal rates.
- `Ugly deflationary depression`: insufficient easing, premature austerity, weak monetization, damaged banks, falling credit, and nominal growth below debt-service needs.
- `Ugly inflationary depression`: excessive money printing, currency confidence collapse, inflationary spiral, or external funding crisis.
- `Unclear`: policy actions and macro response conflict.

## Common Misreads

- Calling policy well managed only because asset prices recover. Check whether income growth, credit quality, unemployment, and debt burdens improve.
- Calling monetization bad by default. In a debt depression, appropriate monetization can be necessary; the issue is scale, targeting, and currency credibility.
- Calling austerity prudent by default. Austerity without adequate easing can deepen the depression.
- Treating all bank rescues as moral hazard. Protect systemic functions while resolving non-systemic failures orderly.
- Treating all failures as healthy discipline. Disorderly systemic failure can turn a recession into depression.

## Suggested Language

Use concise, evidence-backed phrasing:

- "Policy management looks well managed: authorities cut rates quickly, provided broad liquidity, sustained fiscal support, protected systemic institutions, and used balance-sheet expansion enough to stabilize nominal growth."
- "Policy management is mixed: liquidity support was fast, but fiscal austerity and slow bank recapitalization offset the central bank's reflation."
- "Policy management looks poor: policymakers ignored the bubble, continued tightening after the top, delayed monetization, and left systemic institutions undercapitalized."
