# Paul Volcker Fed Decision-Making Case, 1970-1984

## Core Lesson

The Volcker case is not a normal "growth weakens, so central bank eases" case. The Fed's reaction function shifted because inflation credibility had been damaged by the stop-go policies of the 1960s and 1970s. Volcker's priority was to bring inflation and inflation expectations under control once and for all, even at the cost of a severe recession.

Always ask:

1. What did the macro indicators say?
2. What did the market expect the Fed to do?
3. What was the Fed's reaction function?
4. Was policy judged against current growth only, or against inflation credibility and long-run expectations?

## Historical Setup

Background:
- In the 1960s and 1970s, the Fed followed stop-go policy.
- During "go" periods, it lowered rates to support employment.
- During "stop" periods, it raised rates when inflation became too high.
- The bias favored employment even if inflation drifted higher.
- The tradeoff became unstable: inflation and unemployment rose together, helped by commodity shocks and rising inflation expectations.
- Inflation reached about 11% in June 1979.

Volcker:
- Appointed Fed chair in August 1979 because of anti-inflation credibility.
- Believed inflation was the main threat to future stability.
- Believed the Fed had a credibility problem after repeatedly backing down.
- Thought failure to carry through would make later disinflation harder and more painful.

Key decision principle:
- Do not evaluate Volcker as if he were minimizing near-term unemployment only.
- Evaluate him as trying to break entrenched inflation expectations and restore the credibility of money.

## Case Windows

### 1970s Stop-Go Build-Up

Main pattern:
- Growth cycles repeatedly recovered and slowed.
- Headline and core inflation trended higher across cycles.
- Unemployment did not solve the inflation problem.
- Commodity and gold strength reflected inflation and credibility stress.
- Long rates rose as inflation expectations became embedded.

Policy interpretation:
- The Fed repeatedly eased when unemployment rose and tightened when inflation worsened.
- Markets learned to expect the Fed to back down under unemployment pressure.
- This made disinflation harder because the public expected high inflation to continue.

### 1979-1980 First Volcker Attempt

Main pattern:
- Volcker tightened to lower inflation and expectations.
- The Carter credit-control program helped trigger a sharp recession in 1980.
- As unemployment rose, the Fed eased.
- This looked like another stop-go retreat to markets.

Policy interpretation:
- The first attempt was insufficient because easing reinforced doubts about anti-inflation commitment.
- This set up the need for a more credible second tightening.

### Late 1980-1982 Renewed Tightening And Recession

Main pattern:
- The Fed tightened again.
- Federal funds approached roughly 20%.
- Long-run interest rates continued rising for a time; 10-year Treasury yields rose from about 11% in October 1980 to above 15% about a year later.
- The economy entered recession in Q3 1981.
- Interest-sensitive sectors such as manufacturing, construction, housing, and investment were pressured.
- Unemployment rose from about 7.4% at the recession start to nearly 10% a year later and near 11% by late 1982.

Policy interpretation:
- Rising long rates despite high short rates suggested markets doubted the Fed would maintain tight policy.
- Volcker refused to back down because easing too early risked validating inflation expectations.
- Congressional pressure to ease was intense, but the Fed judged the long-run cost of retreat as larger.

### 1982-1984 Easing, Rebound, And Inflation Scares

Main pattern:
- By October 1982, inflation had fallen to around 5%.
- Long-run rates began to decline.
- The Fed allowed the funds rate to fall back near 9%.
- Unemployment fell quickly from near 11% at the end of 1982 to around 8% one year later.
- Growth and activity rebounded strongly.
- Inflation later picked up modestly, creating inflation scares.
- The Fed kept rates relatively steady at first, then tightened modestly as inflation rose.

Policy interpretation:
- Many blind case readers expect continued easing after recession and rebound.
- Volcker did not choose to loosen because the top priority remained preventing inflation from re-accelerating.
- If rates rose about as much as inflation, real policy rates may have stayed roughly unchanged; nominal tightening did not necessarily mean real tightening.
- Equities and bonds sold off around the modest tightening, but later benefited as inflation credibility improved and a multi-decade decline in rates began.

## Indicator Notes From The Charts

### Growth Versus Potential

Observed pattern:
- Growth was volatile across 1970-1984.
- Growth ran above potential during recoveries and fell sharply in recessions.
- After the 1981-1982 recession, growth rebounded strongly by 1983-1984.
- Potential growth moved much more slowly than actual growth.

Policy read:
- A normal short-cycle rule might say strong post-recession growth supports normalization but not aggressive tightening.
- In the Volcker case, strong growth after disinflation created room to avoid premature easing.
- The Fed cared whether growth would reignite inflation before credibility was secure.

### Headline And Core Inflation

Observed pattern:
- Headline and core inflation surged in the mid-1970s.
- Inflation eased, then surged again around 1979-1981.
- Inflation fell sharply by 1982-1983.
- A modest pick-up occurred after the rebound.

Policy read:
- Inflation was the decisive indicator.
- The key was not one month's inflation but entrenched inflation expectations after a decade of stop-go policy.
- A modest rebound in inflation after the recession mattered more than it would in a normal cycle because credibility was not fully restored.

### Industrial Production

Observed pattern:
- Industrial production rose through expansions.
- It fell around the mid-1970s recession and again around 1980-1982.
- It rebounded strongly by 1983-1984.

Policy read:
- Strong industrial rebound supported the view that the economy could tolerate a firmer anti-inflation stance.
- Weak activity alone was not enough to force easing while inflation credibility was at stake.

### Unemployment

Observed pattern:
- Unemployment rose sharply in the mid-1970s.
- It declined later in the 1970s.
- It rose dramatically in the 1981-1982 recession, approaching 11%.
- It then fell quickly by 1983-1984.

Policy read:
- Normally, high unemployment pushes central banks to ease.
- Volcker accepted high unemployment as the cost of breaking inflation expectations.
- Once unemployment was falling and growth rebounding, the case for further easing weakened.

### Short Rate, Long Rate, And Yield Curve

Observed pattern:
- Short rates were volatile and rose sharply under Volcker, approaching 20%.
- Long rates rose too, reaching very high levels, instead of immediately falling in response to anti-inflation policy.
- The yield curve inverted or became deeply negative during tight policy, then steepened as the Fed eased and long rates began responding to lower inflation.

Policy read:
- Long rates rising while short rates were high signaled markets doubted the Fed's commitment or expected persistent inflation.
- Volcker needed to maintain credibility until long rates and inflation expectations broke.
- A modest nominal rate hike after inflation rose may have kept real rates about steady rather than truly tightening.

### Private Credit Creation

Observed pattern:
- Private credit creation was strong in expansions.
- It dropped around recessions.
- It rebounded strongly in recoveries.

Policy read:
- Rebounding private credit after the 1981-1982 recession showed easing had traction.
- Too much easing risked rebuilding inflationary demand before credibility was secure.

### Government Credit Creation

Observed pattern:
- Government credit creation rose during weak periods and fiscal stress.
- It was elevated around recessions and fiscal deterioration.

Policy read:
- Fiscal support and deficits could offset monetary restraint, but also complicated inflation credibility.
- The Fed could not rely on fiscal discipline to anchor inflation expectations.

### Real Household Consumption Growth

Observed pattern:
- Consumption weakened during recessions.
- It rebounded sharply after easing and disinflation.

Policy read:
- Strong rebound in consumption argued against continued emergency easing.
- Consumption recovery plus modest inflation pick-up supported holding or modest tightening.

### Real Residential Investment Growth

Observed pattern:
- Residential investment was very interest-rate-sensitive.
- It contracted under tight policy.
- It rebounded when rates fell.

Policy read:
- Housing pain was an intended transmission channel of tight money.
- The Fed tolerated housing weakness because inflation credibility was the priority.
- After housing rebounded, continued easing risked overstimulating a rate-sensitive sector.

### Real Business Investment Growth

Observed pattern:
- Business investment weakened in downturns.
- It rebounded strongly by 1983-1984.

Policy read:
- Investment recovery showed the economy was responding to lower rates and restored confidence.
- Strong investment reduced the urgency to loosen further.

### Savings Rate

Observed pattern:
- Savings fluctuated and generally declined into the early 1980s.
- It moved with household stress, income changes, and confidence.

Policy read:
- Falling savings alongside consumption recovery can signal demand strength.
- Demand strength mattered because the Fed feared reigniting inflation before expectations were anchored.

### Equity Index

Observed pattern:
- Equities suffered during tightening/recessions.
- Equities rallied strongly after the disinflation turn and recovery.
- Equities and bonds sold off during inflation scares and modest renewed tightening.

Policy read:
- Near-term equity weakness did not automatically imply the Fed should ease.
- Once inflation credibility improved, equities benefited from falling long rates and better growth.

### Government Bonds At Equity Volatility

Observed pattern:
- Bonds sold off as rates and inflation expectations rose.
- Bonds later rallied as inflation came under control and long rates fell.
- Bonds could sell off again during inflation scares.

Policy read:
- Bond market behavior was central to the credibility problem.
- The Fed needed long rates to believe disinflation was durable.

### Commodities

Observed pattern:
- Commodities surged through inflationary 1970s and around the second inflation wave.
- They weakened after disinflation gained traction.

Policy read:
- Commodity strength reinforced inflation pressure and inflation expectations.
- Commodity weakness later confirmed that tight policy was working.

### Gold

Observed pattern:
- Gold rose dramatically during the inflation/credibility crisis, peaking around the early 1980s.
- Gold fell after disinflation credibility improved.

Policy read:
- Gold was a market signal of distrust in money and inflation credibility.
- Falling gold helped confirm that Volcker's credibility strategy was working.

### Current Account

Observed pattern:
- Current account fluctuated near balance in the broader chart window.
- External balance was not the main driver of the Volcker decision.

Policy read:
- The case was primarily domestic inflation credibility, not a classic balance-of-payments defense.

### Fiscal Deficit

Observed pattern:
- Fiscal deficits widened during downturns and early-1980s policy mix.
- Deficits remained a background pressure.

Policy read:
- Fiscal deficits could put upward pressure on long rates.
- The Fed's anti-inflation credibility was even more important if fiscal policy was not strongly disinflationary.

### Home Prices

Observed pattern:
- Home price growth slowed sharply during tight policy and the 1981-1982 recession.
- Home price growth recovered by 1983-1984.

Policy read:
- Housing sensitivity showed tight policy was biting.
- Recovery in home prices after easing argued against further emergency loosening.

## Why Volcker Did Not Choose To Loosen

Use this logic when explaining the decision:

1. The Fed had a credibility problem after years of stop-go policy.
2. Inflation expectations were entrenched; easing too early would confirm the market's belief that the Fed would back down.
3. Long rates rose even when short rates were very high, showing credibility was not yet secure.
4. Volcker believed failing to carry through would create worse economic conditions later.
5. The post-recession rebound in growth, consumption, credit, housing, and investment reduced the need for further easing.
6. A modest pick-up in inflation after the rebound justified a bias toward holding or tightening.
7. If nominal rates rose roughly with inflation, real rates may not have tightened much.
8. Near-term equity and bond weakness was acceptable if the long-run payoff was lower inflation and lower long rates.

Short version:
- Volcker did not ease because the Fed was not optimizing the next few quarters of growth. It was restoring the credibility of the currency and the inflation anchor.

## Decision Rules

If inflation is still high or inflation expectations are not anchored:
- Do not recommend easing solely because growth is weak or unemployment is high.
- Check whether easing would repeat stop-go policy and damage credibility.

If long rates rise despite high short rates:
- Treat it as a sign that markets doubt disinflation credibility.
- Tight policy may need to persist until long rates begin to fall.

If growth rebounds after disinflation:
- Do not assume the central bank should keep easing.
- Check whether inflation is picking up and whether credibility remains fragile.

If nominal rates rise with inflation:
- Evaluate real rates, not just nominal rates.
- A nominal hike can be neutral in real terms if inflation rises by a similar amount.

## Market Read-Through

Near term:
- Equities and bonds can sell off when the Fed tightens or resists easing.
- Rate-sensitive sectors such as housing, construction, manufacturing, and investment can weaken.
- Commodities and gold can rise if inflation credibility is weak.

Medium to long term:
- If inflation credibility is restored, long rates can fall.
- Bonds can rally as inflation expectations decline.
- Equities can benefit from lower discount rates and more stable macro conditions.
- This can set up a long period of lower inflation, lower rates, and stronger asset returns.

## Common Mistakes

- Mistaking a growth rebound for a reason to keep easing.
- Ignoring the Fed's reaction function and treating all central banks as growth maximizers.
- Looking only at unemployment and not inflation expectations.
- Looking only at short rates and ignoring long rates as a credibility signal.
- Calling a nominal rate increase "tightening" without checking real rates.
- Assuming weak equities and bonds mean policy was wrong; the policy may be buying long-term credibility at short-term market cost.

## Suggested Language

Use concise explanations:

- "Volcker did not ease because the Fed's binding constraint was credibility, not near-term growth. After years of stop-go policy, easing into every rise in unemployment would have validated inflation expectations."
- "The key signal was that long rates kept rising even as short rates approached extreme levels. Markets did not yet believe the Fed would stay tight long enough to break inflation."
- "The later modest rate increase was not necessarily a large real tightening because inflation also rose. The Fed was leaning against an inflation scare while preserving hard-won credibility."
