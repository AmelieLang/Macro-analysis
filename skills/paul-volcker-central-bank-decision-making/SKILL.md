# Paul Volcker_Central Bank Decision Making

## Overview

Use this skill to analyze the 1970-1984 U.S. Volcker Fed case as a central-bank reaction-function case study. The key lesson is that macro conditions alone were not enough: policymakers' credibility problem and anti-inflation preference changed the meaning of the same indicators.

## Workflow

1. Load `references/volcker-case-rubric.md`.
2. Identify the exact window being discussed:
   - 1970s stop-go inflation build-up.
   - 1979-1980 first Volcker tightening and credit-control recession.
   - 1980-1982 renewed tightening and deep recession.
   - 1982-1984 easing, rebound, and inflation-scare period.
3. Gather or inspect indicators:
   - Growth versus potential and industrial production.
   - Headline and core inflation.
   - Unemployment.
   - Short rate, long rate, and yield curve.
   - Private and government credit creation.
   - Real household consumption, residential investment, business investment, and savings rate.
   - Equities, government bonds, commodities, gold, current account, fiscal deficit, and home prices.
4. Infer the central-bank reaction function before recommending or judging policy.
5. Explain why Volcker did or did not ease by weighing inflation credibility, inflation expectations, real rates, unemployment, market expectations, and long-run costs.
6. State the likely market read-through, including why equities and bonds could sell off near tightening but do well later once inflation credibility improved.

## Guardrails

- Do not mechanically apply a normal short-term debt-cycle rule that recession means immediate easing. In this case, inflation credibility dominated the reaction function.
- Separate nominal rates from real rates. A rate increase that matches an inflation increase may leave real policy rates roughly unchanged.
- Watch long rates: if long rates rise despite high short rates, markets may believe the Fed will back down and inflation will persist.
- Distinguish bad near-term asset performance from the longer-term payoff of restored inflation credibility.
- Do not present the policy or market assessment as financial advice.
