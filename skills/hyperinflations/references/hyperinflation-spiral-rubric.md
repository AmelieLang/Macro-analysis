# Hyperinflation Spiral Rubric

## Core Definition

Hyperinflation is a subset of inflationary depressions in which goods and services prices more than double every year or worse, wealth is destroyed, and money begins losing its core functions. The classic driver is an unresolved external and fiscal/debt-service imbalance funded repeatedly by money printing.

Use the classic monthly 50 percent inflation threshold only as an additional severe marker. The skill's broader trigger is annual price doubling plus currency, money, and behavioral breakdown evidence.

## Necessary Background Conditions

Hyperinflation risk is highest when several of these are present:

- Non-reserve currency or collapsing reserve-currency confidence.
- Large external spending needs relative to external income.
- Heavy external debt service, reparations, imported energy/food bill, or foreign-currency debt.
- Low or depleted FX reserves.
- Fiscal deficit that is hard to cut politically or socially.
- Central bank financing of government, banks, businesses, or external spending.
- Capital flight and currency depreciation already underway.
- Weak inflation credibility from prior inflation, default, controls, confiscation, or currency losses.

## Trigger Conditions

Score each condition as `yes`, `mixed`, `no`, or `unknown`.

### 1. External Imbalance Does Not Close

Ask whether exports, external income, imports, debt service, and capital flows are still inconsistent.

Warning signs:

- Current account/trade deficit remains large despite devaluation.
- Imports and debt service cannot be financed by exports, reserves, or stable capital inflows.
- External debt service consumes a large share of exports or reserves.
- Policymakers delay spending cuts or restructuring to maintain domestic demand.

### 2. External Spending Is Funded By Printing Money

The most important hyperinflation marker is repeated monetization of an imbalance that should be closed by adjustment, default, restructuring, or external financing.

Warning signs:

- Monetary base or broad money accelerates because the central bank funds fiscal deficits, bank rescues, businesses, or quasi-fiscal losses.
- Government relies on central bank credit because private creditors flee.
- Money printing finances imported goods, external payments, subsidies, or attempts to maintain unsustainable living standards.
- Policymakers cannot stop printing without causing extreme liquidity shortage and economic collapse.

### 3. Depreciation, Inflation, And Capital Flight Reinforce Each Other

Warning signs:

- Currency declines inspire more capital flight.
- Capital flight forces more depreciation or reserve loss.
- Depreciation raises import prices and inflation.
- Inflation and negative real rates push savers out of local money.
- More money printing is used to maintain commerce after prices rise.

### 4. Inflation Psychology And Wage-Price Spiral Set In

Warning signs:

- Wage demands rise to preserve purchasing power.
- Producers raise prices to cover rising wage and input costs.
- Wages, rents, contracts, taxes, or benefits become indexed to inflation or foreign currency.
- Price changes become more frequent.
- Households spend quickly because cash loses value.

### 5. Printed Money Stops Stimulating Growth

Early currency declines can help exports and nominal activity. In the hyperinflation spiral, printing becomes less effective.

Warning signs:

- Each money-printing round buys less real output.
- Printed money moves into foreign currency, gold, commodities, property, inventories, or imported goods rather than productive investment.
- Equities no longer preserve hard-currency wealth even if they rise in local-currency terms.
- Domestic capital investment is made as a store of value rather than because capacity is needed.

### 6. Debt Maturities Shorten And Liquidity Breaks

Warning signs:

- Depositors shift from term deposits to demand deposits, cash, or foreign currency.
- Lenders shorten duration or stop lending.
- Average debt maturity falls.
- Banks cannot meet demand for cash or hard currency.
- Businesses cannot transact because currency or credit is unavailable.
- Central bank prints directly for banks or businesses to avoid commerce halting.

### 7. Money Functions Break Down

Hyperinflation is confirmed when money loses its roles.

Store of value:

- People hold only days of cash balances.
- Savings flee to hard currency, commodities, gold, property, inventories, or barter goods.

Unit of account:

- Prices are quoted in foreign currency, indexed units, goods, or constantly changing local prices.
- Too many zeros make accounting unreliable.

Medium of exchange:

- Sellers refuse local currency, demand foreign currency, barter, or immediate repricing.
- Shortages, store closures, and commerce breakdown become visible.

## Stages

### Stage 1: Inflationary Deleveraging

Currency weakens, inflation rises, debt burdens grow, and policymakers face a tradeoff between defending the currency and supporting the economy.

### Stage 2: Repeated Monetization

Policymakers print to cover deficits, bank liquidity, businesses, or external spending because adjustment is socially, politically, or economically painful.

### Stage 3: Inflation Psychology

Workers, firms, savers, banks, and investors change behavior. They expect depreciation and inflation, shorten maturities, index contracts, and flee the currency faster.

### Stage 4: Hyperinflation

Money loses store-of-value and medium-of-exchange functions. Printing cannot solve the foreign-exchange and real-goods shortage. Output contracts sharply, financial wealth is wiped out, and social order deteriorates.

### Stage 5: Currency Replacement Or Hard Stabilization

The old currency rarely recovers as a store of wealth. Classic stabilization requires a new currency or monetary regime with hard backing, credible fiscal/external adjustment, and a phase-out of the old currency.

## Scoring

```text
0-1 yes: no hyperinflation signal unless inflation is already extreme.
2-3 yes: watchlist; inflationary deleveraging has hyperinflation ingredients.
4-5 yes: high risk; spiral dynamics are active.
6 yes: spiraling; hyperinflation is likely unless policy changes quickly.
7 yes or money functions already breaking down: hyperinflation.
Unknown-heavy: insufficient data; identify missing inflation, currency, money, and behavioral series.
```

Override rules:

- If money is already failing as a medium of exchange, classify as `hyperinflation` even if some data are missing.
- If inflation is extreme but the external/fiscal imbalance has closed and money functions are stabilizing, classify as `post-hyperinflation repair` or `stabilizing`, not ongoing hyperinflation.
- If annual inflation exceeds 100 percent but currency and money-function breakdown evidence is weak, classify as `high inflation/high risk`, not automatically hyperinflation.

## Policy Stabilization Checklist

A credible exit usually requires several of:

- External imbalance closure: imports, external debt service, and external income brought into line.
- Fiscal balance credible enough to stop central bank financing.
- Default, restructuring, or external support when foreign-currency obligations are impossible.
- Bank and business liquidity handled without open-ended money printing.
- Positive real rates or credible hard backing to restore local-currency demand.
- Capital flight slowed by credibility, not only coercion.
- New currency or hard-backed monetary anchor when the old currency has lost trust.

## Asset Behavior

Typical pattern:

- Local currency: collapses as store of value.
- Local nominal bonds: wiped out in real/hard-currency terms.
- Equities: can rise in local currency early but often fail to keep pace with depreciation and inflation as hyperinflation takes hold.
- Gold, commodities, foreign currency, and commodity-linked assets: preferred stores of value.
- Real assets: may preserve value but can be impaired by confiscation, taxes, controls, social disorder, and illiquidity.

State this as historical asset behavior, not personalized investment advice.
