## 1. Three Operating Principles

Ray's investment approach can be summarized as:

- Fundamental: build from the economic machine, not from surface price action.
- Systematic: translate the logic into repeatable cause-effect templates.
- Diversified: avoid relying on one forecast; balance exposures across environments.

The analyst's job is to connect bottom-up transactions with top-down macro forces, then compare market-implied expectations with a reasoned view of what is likely.

## 2. Transaction-Based Pricing

Use the identity:

`Price = total spending / total quantity sold`

In shorthand:

- Total spending = money + credit.
- Quantity sold = goods, services, financial assets, debt issued, shares outstanding, inventories, production, or float available for sale.
- Price changes when total spending changes, quantity sold changes, or both.

For macro aggregates:

| Concept | Transaction lens |
| --- | --- |
| Nominal GDP | money + credit spent |
| Real GDP | quantity sold |
| CPI / price level | price paid |
| Nominal growth | change in total spending |
| Real growth | change in quantity sold |
| Inflation | change in price |

Total spending usually moves faster than quantity because money and credit can be created, withdrawn, or redirected quickly. Quantity often moves more slowly, although supply shocks, inventory swings, issuance, buybacks, defaults, and production outages can be decisive in specific markets.

## 3. Money, Credit, and Spending Power

Treat credit as spending power because borrowers can buy now by promising to deliver money later. This matters because:

- Credit expansion lifts spending, activity, asset prices, collateral values, and often further borrowing capacity.
- Credit contraction reduces spending, asset prices, collateral values, and refinancing capacity.
- Central banks influence credit through short rates, liquidity facilities, asset purchases, collateral policy, and expectations.
- Governments influence spending through fiscal deficits, transfers, taxation, guarantees, issuance, and regulation.

When analyzing demand, ask:

- Is buying funded by cash, income, retained earnings, borrowing, margin, or portfolio rebalancing?
- Are lenders more willing or less willing to create credit?
- Are collateral values rising or falling?
- Are debt-service burdens rising or falling?
- Are buyers price-sensitive, forced, policy-driven, benchmark-driven, or speculative?

## 4. Difference From Conventional Supply/Demand Framing

Use these distinctions:

- Measure demand as dollars spent, not just units desired.
- Disaggregate buyers and sellers instead of treating the market as one theoretical buyer and seller.
- Attribute rallies and selloffs to actual cohorts and flows where possible.
- Treat much of what is often called "velocity" as credit creation or contraction.
- Use credit dynamics to explain cycles, bubbles, deleveragings, and why money printing can be inflationary in one context but not another.

## 5. Linking Transactions to Theoretical Value

Financial assets exchange a lump sum today for future cash flows. Their prices therefore embed expectations for:

- Future real growth.
- Future inflation.
- Risk premiums.
- Discount rates.
- Default probability and recovery value where relevant.
- Currency debasement or currency scarcity where relevant.

Transactions are the mechanism that moves prices; valuation is the future picture those prices imply. Good analysis uses both lenses:

- Transaction lens: who bought or sold, with what money or credit, against what supply?
- Valuation lens: what future does the new price imply, and is that future likely?

Opportunity exists when market-implied expectations differ from a well-supported view of future conditions.

## 6. Fundamental Drivers of Asset Returns

Every major asset class is a package of exposures to:

- Discounted growth.
- Discounted inflation.
- Risk premiums.
- Discount rates.

Big price moves usually come from surprises that change expectations, not simply from realized data matching what was already priced.

### Nominal Government Bonds

Nominal bond yields can be decomposed into:

- Real yield: real return / real cost of capital.
- Break-even inflation: market-implied inflation compensation.
- Risk and term premiums.

Bond prices rise when yields fall. Bonds usually benefit from weaker-than-expected growth and lower-than-expected inflation because those conditions reduce real yields, inflation compensation, or expected policy rates.

### Inflation-Linked Bonds

Inflation-linked bonds are driven heavily by real yields and realized inflation compensation. They often benefit when real yields fall and can help when inflation realization exceeds what nominal assets were prepared for.

### Equities

Equities are claims on future earnings. They are usually helped by stronger-than-expected growth because revenue and margins improve, but hurt by rising discount rates and risk premiums. Earnings often move more than nominal GDP because corporate profits are leveraged to the economy through operating and financial leverage.

### Credit Spreads

Corporate spreads reflect compensation for default risk and liquidity risk over safer government bonds. Stronger growth usually supports revenues, margins, and debt service. Spreads can widen violently in recessions, liquidity shocks, or periods of falling collateral values.

### Commodities

Commodities usually benefit from stronger-than-expected growth and higher-than-expected inflation. They can also move for idiosyncratic supply reasons because short-run supply is often inelastic.

### Gold

Gold is less tied to industrial growth than most commodities. It often acts as protection against fiat debasement, monetary inflation, negative real yields, policy credibility loss, and currency stress.

### Emerging-Market Hard-Currency Debt

EM hard-currency spreads reflect sovereign default risk in a currency the issuer cannot print. Exporters often benefit from stronger global growth and higher commodity prices, while importers may be hurt by the same shock.

## 7. Environmental Bias Map

Use this map as a starting point, then adjust for valuation, leverage, policy, and supply:

| Asset class | Growth expectations rising | Growth expectations falling | Inflation expectations rising | Inflation expectations falling |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Equities | Generally positive | Generally negative | Mixed/negative if margins and rates suffer | Often positive if margins and discount rates improve |
| Nominal bonds | Usually negative | Usually positive | Negative | Positive |
| Inflation-linked bonds | Mixed; real yields may rise | Often positive through falling real yields | Positive through inflation protection | Mixed/negative |
| Commodities | Positive | Negative | Positive | Negative |
| Gold | Unreliable | Often positive if policy eases or real yields fall | Positive if fiat debasement risk rises | Mixed |
| Corporate credit spreads | Positive/tighter | Negative/wider | Mixed | Mixed; dangerous if deflation raises defaults |
| EM hard-currency debt | Positive/tighter | Negative/wider | Positive for exporters, negative for importers | Mixed |

Risk premiums and discount rates affect nearly all assets in the same direction: rising discount rates or risk premiums pressure prices lower; falling discount rates or risk premiums support prices.

## 8. Diagnosing Macro Events From Asset Returns

When the user gives returns for several assets and asks what environment or macro event fits, infer the four driver axes first:

- Growth expectations: rising or falling.
- Inflation expectations: rising or falling.
- Risk premiums: expanding or contracting.
- Discount rates: rising or falling.

Use cross-asset logic:

| Pattern | Likely driver mix | Typical macro interpretation |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Equities up, commodities up, bonds down | Growth up, inflation up, discount rates up, risk premiums often contracting | Reflationary recovery, fiscal/monetary stimulus, reopening, demand recovery |
| Equities down, commodities down, bonds up | Growth down, inflation down, discount rates down, risk premiums expanding | Deflationary risk-off shock, financial crisis, demand collapse |
| Equities down, bonds down, commodities up | Inflation up and discount rates up, growth hit by real-income squeeze, risk premiums may expand | Stagflationary or supply-shock environment |
| Equities up, bonds up, commodities mixed/down | Growth expectations stable or improving, inflation down, discount rates down, risk premiums contracting | Disinflationary easing or soft landing |
| Gold up, commodities up, bonds mixed/down | Monetary inflation, currency-debasement fear, falling real yields or inflation protection demand | Fiat confidence shock, aggressive money printing, geopolitical or reserve-currency stress |
| Credit spreads wider, equities down, government bonds up | Default risk/risk premiums up, flight to safety, growth down | Credit event, banking stress, recession scare |

Do not pick the historical event first and force the assets to fit it. Identify the driver pattern, then match events that would logically create that pattern.

Example: stock +6%, bonds -2%, commodities +10%.

- Commodities and equities both rise while bonds fall.
- Growth expectations are likely revised up.
- Inflation expectations are likely revised up.
- Discount rates are likely rising, which hurts bonds and offsets some equity upside.
- Risk premiums are likely contracting if equities rise despite rate pressure.
- A good event match is a reflationary recovery with stimulus and improved risk appetite, such as vaccine rollout plus fiscal/monetary stimulus in early 2021.
- Poor matches include a pure deflationary default or a risk-off geopolitical shock because those would usually hurt equities and often support safe bonds.

Example: stock -9%, bonds +2%, commodities -11%.

- Equities and commodities fall sharply while bonds rise.
- Growth expectations are likely revised down.
- Inflation expectations are likely revised down.
- Discount rates are likely falling as central banks cut and investors seek safety.
- Risk premiums are likely expanding sharply.
- A good event match is a systemic deflationary risk-off shock, such as the Lehman Brothers collapse in September 2008.
- Poor matches include growth-positive stimulus because that would usually lift equities and commodities.

When an asset's move conflicts with its normal environmental bias, explain which force dominated. For example, equities can rise while inflation and discount rates rise if growth expectations and contracting risk premiums dominate. Bonds can rise during a crisis even as risk premiums rise if the fall in growth, inflation, and risk-free rates dominates.

## 9. Debt-Cycle and Liquidity Checklist

Look for the self-reinforcing loop:

1. Easier money and credit increase spending.
2. Higher spending lifts income, asset prices, and collateral values.
3. Higher collateral and confidence support more borrowing.
4. Debt burdens eventually rise faster than income.
5. Tightening, income disappointment, or collateral declines reverse the loop.
6. Forced selling and credit contraction reduce spending and prices.
7. Policymakers may cut rates, provide liquidity, guarantee debts, run deficits, or print money.

Classify the environment:

- Normal expansion: income growth supports debt service.
- Bubble: extrapolated expectations plus leverage and broad buyer participation.
- Top: tightening, stretched valuations, rising debt service, or supply response meets weakening demand.
- Deleveraging: debt service and credit contraction suppress spending.
- Reflation: public money creation offsets private credit contraction.

## 10. Bubble Checklist

Flag bubble risk when several are true:

- Prices discount highly optimistic future conditions.
- Buying is increasingly financed with debt or margin.
- New buyers enter because prices have risen.
- Supply issuance increases to meet speculative demand.
- Lending standards deteriorate.
- Cash flows do not justify prices without heroic assumptions.
- Policy is accommodative despite rising leverage or inflation.
- The asset is treated as safe because recent performance has been strong.

## 11. Return Streams and the Holy Grail

Think of every investment as a return stream. Do not be constrained by labels like stocks, bonds, commodities, private equity, hedge funds, or currencies. Judge each return stream by three questions:

1. How good is the expected return?
2. How risky is it?
3. How well does it diversify the other return streams in the portfolio?

The goal is not simply to find the best single investment. The bigger edge is often engineering a better portfolio by combining good return streams that do not move together.

Diversification only works when return streams are meaningfully uncorrelated. Owning many stocks is not enough if they share the same equity beta and fall together in the same environments. A mix of return streams that wiggle at different times can materially improve return per unit of risk.

Ray's "Holy Grail" idea:

- A handful of correlated return streams still leaves the portfolio dominated by the same underlying risk.
- As the number of good, low-correlation or uncorrelated return streams rises, portfolio volatility falls faster than expected return.
- Around 15 good, uncorrelated return streams can greatly improve the return-to-risk ratio, potentially by several times.
- This means a great portfolio does not require every component to be great. Good and uncorrelated can beat great but concentrated.

Use this logic when assessing portfolio quality:

| Question | Good sign | Bad sign |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Return | Positive expected return or clear alpha edge | Return depends on one heroic forecast |
| Risk | Risk is understood and sized intentionally | Hidden leverage, liquidity risk, or left-tail exposure |
| Diversification | Low or negative correlation to existing risks | Same growth, equity, credit, liquidity, or duration beta in a different wrapper |
| Efficiency | Attractive return per unit of risk | High return only because risk is high |

The best portfolio is usually not the one with the highest raw return. It is the one with the best return per unit of risk. A more efficient portfolio can be adjusted with leverage or de-risking to target higher return, lower risk, or a better balance of both.

## 12. Total Return: Risk-Free Rate, Beta, and Alpha

Decompose any investment or portfolio:

`Total return = risk-free return + beta return + alpha return`

Where:

- Risk-free return is the return from the investor's risk-neutral position. Unless stated otherwise, use short-term government bills or cash in the investor's local currency.
- Beta return is compensation for passively holding an asset class or systematic risk exposure.
- Alpha return is return from active skill: security selection, timing, relative-value trades, manager selection, or a trading rule that beats what is already priced.

Use the arithmetic discipline:

- To raise excess return, take more beta risk, take more alpha risk, improve beta efficiency, or improve alpha efficiency.
- There are no magic extra levers. The question is which risks the investor is comfortable taking and whether those risks are diversified.
- Explicitly separate the beta a manager gives from the alpha the manager adds. Do not confuse being long a rising market with skill.

## 13. Beta Versus Alpha

Beta:

- Often seen as market risk.
- Has a logical basis for a risk premium because investors transfer risk to buyers who require compensation.
- Is limited in number because there are only so many viable asset classes.
- Is typically correlated with other betas.
- Is cheap and easy to access through passive vehicles.
- Should outperform cash over long horizons because investors require risk premiums and the capitalist system depends on risk capital earning more than cash.
- Usually offers modest excess return relative to risk, often roughly 0.2 to 0.3 return-to-risk over long periods.
- Is affected by two timeless forces: collecting risk premium over time and cyclical changes in discounted growth, inflation, risk premiums, and discount rates.

Alpha:

- Is a bet against other market participants.
- Is zero sum before costs and negative sum after transaction costs and fees.
- Has no automatic risk premium unless there is identifiable skill or a structural transfer of risk.
- Can come from many sources, strategies, and managers.
- Can be more diversifying than betas if the alphas are genuinely independent.
- Can be highly positive or highly negative depending on skill.
- Is expensive to access and difficult to predict.

Practical rule:

- If there is an identifiable transfer of risk and a broad class of investors is paid to bear it, analyze it as beta.
- If the return depends on being smarter, faster, more disciplined, or structurally advantaged versus other investors, analyze it as alpha.
- If the "alpha" is a well-known idea that is already priced into markets, treat it as likely poor alpha.

Many traditional managers package beta and alpha together. A Ray-style analysis separates them so the portfolio can choose the best betas and the best alphas independently.

## 14. Private Markets

Analyze private markets by decomposing them into public-market-like betas plus leverage, liquidity, smoothing, and manager/deal alpha.

Private equity:

- Often resembles levered public equity, especially small-cap or sector-relevant equity, with reported returns smoothed because assets are not marked to market continuously.
- Expected premium may reflect higher leverage, liquidity risk, operational improvements, deal selection, governance, and manager skill.
- Do not assume all excess return is alpha. First ask how much is equity beta, leverage beta, sector beta, and illiquidity premium.

Venture capital:

- Often resembles a levered and smoothed exposure to growth, technology, or innovation-heavy public equities, plus very high dispersion from deal selection.
- The beta-like component can be proxied by growth-heavy public markets; the alpha component comes from access, selection, timing, governance, and portfolio construction.

Private credit:

- Often combines credit beta, illiquidity premium, underwriting skill, collateral selection, leverage, and exposure to the debt cycle.
- Watch refinancing risk, covenant quality, default losses, manager marks, and whether reported smoothness hides economic volatility.

When comparing private and public assets, ask:

- What public market proxy has similar environmental biases?
- How much leverage is embedded?
- How much smoothing is due to appraisal or accounting?
- What compensation is for illiquidity rather than skill?
- What is true alpha after beta, leverage, and smoothing are accounted for?

## 15. Foreign Assets and Currencies

A foreign asset has two separable return streams:

`Foreign asset return = hedged local asset return + currency return`

The hedged asset can have a positive expected return because it contains the local risk-free rate and asset risk premium. The currency exposure is different:

- Currency crosses generally have near-zero expected return over long horizons.
- Currencies can be very volatile over shorter horizons.
- A currency can still add value if it diversifies the portfolio, especially if it is negatively correlated with the rest of the portfolio.
- A small FX exposure only needs modest negative correlation to be useful.
- A large FX exposure requires a much more negative and reliable correlation to justify its zero expected return and added volatility.

When analyzing a foreign asset, separate:

- The local asset beta.
- The local risk-free rate.
- The local asset risk premium.
- The FX exposure.
- The FX hedge decision.
- Any active currency alpha.

## 17. Tone

Be direct, causal, and structured. Prefer "this buyer cohort has more credit to spend against constrained supply" over "sentiment improved." Use the word "sentiment" only after explaining the money, credit, quantity, and expectation channels that make sentiment matter.
