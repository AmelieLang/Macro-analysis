# Bubble Indicator Catalog

## Scoring

Score every check as:

- `yes`: several indicators point clearly to bubble behavior for the country/market/year.
- `mixed`: evidence is partial, asset-specific, contradictory, or justified by fundamentals.
- `no`: the best available indicators do not support the check.
- `unknown`: official data are missing or too stale to judge.

Summary thresholds:

```text
6-7 yes: Strong bubble
4-5 yes, or 3 yes plus several mixed: Probable bubble
2-3 yes: Watchlist
0-1 yes: Weak bubble evidence
Unknown-heavy matrix: Insufficient data or low confidence
```

Treat `mixed` as half-evidence for synthesis, but do not mechanically add scores without reading the pattern.

## The Seven Checks

### 1. Prices High Relative To Traditional Measures

Core question: are asset prices expensive versus history and fundamentals?

Official indicators to seek:

- Equity market capitalization to GDP.
- Equity price index versus earnings, book value, dividends, sales, or trend nominal GDP when official exchange/regulator data exist.
- Residential property price index, real house price index, price-to-income ratio, and price-to-rent ratio.
- Commercial property price index and capitalization rates where official.
- Credit spreads, risk premia, or sovereign/corporate yield spreads where official.

Score `yes` when several valuation anchors are far above the country's own history or peer norms.

### 2. Prices Discount Future Rapid Appreciation

Core question: do prices require extrapolating rapid price gains, unusually high growth, or unusually easy money?

Official indicators to seek:

- Multi-year asset-price growth relative to nominal GDP, rents, incomes, or earnings.
- Real house price appreciation versus household income growth.
- Equity market value growth versus earnings, dividends, and nominal GDP growth.
- Survey or supervisory evidence that buyers expect continued price gains.
- Low implied risk premia or compressed spreads relative to debt and macro risk.

Score `yes` when prices appear sustainable only if rapid appreciation, peak margins, low rates, or easy credit continue.

### 3. Purchases Financed By High Leverage

Core question: is borrowed money materially financing purchases?

Official indicators to seek:

- Private non-financial credit to GDP and credit-to-GDP gap.
- Household debt to GDP or disposable income.
- Non-financial corporate debt to GDP or profits.
- Debt service ratios for households, corporates, and private non-financial sector.
- Mortgage credit growth, loan-to-value ratios, debt-to-income ratios, interest-only loans, or investor mortgages.
- Margin debt, securities financing, repo, broker-dealer receivables, derivatives exposure, or leveraged-fund data where official.
- Bank and non-bank financial leverage, shadow banking, and foreign-currency borrowing.

Score `yes` when asset demand, income growth, or market liquidity depends materially on rising debt.

### 4. Buyers Or Companies Making Forward Purchases

Core question: is demand pulled forward because buyers fear future price increases or scarcity?

Official indicators to seek:

- Housing transaction volumes, pre-sales, off-plan sales, speculative purchases, or investor-buyer shares.
- Building permits, housing starts, completions, land purchases, inventories, and vacancy rates.
- Corporate inventory accumulation, commodity stockpiling, capex, land banks, M&A, or buybacks that depend on continued boom conditions.
- Durable goods or capital goods purchases ahead of tax, rate, regulation, or price changes.
- Import volumes for bubble-related capital goods or construction materials.

Score `yes` when official quantity data show demand materially accelerated from the future into the bubble period.

### 5. New Participants Entered The Market

Core question: have marginal or nontraditional buyers broadened the boom?

Official indicators to seek:

- New brokerage, securities, futures, fund, or digital trading accounts from regulators/exchanges.
- Listed company count, IPO count/value, new fund launches, or securities issuance.
- First-time homebuyer shares, investor mortgages, foreign buyer shares, or nonresident property purchases.
- New lenders, mortgage brokers, securitization channels, fintech platforms, or shadow-bank balance sheets.
- Household participation in equities, mutual funds, pensions, or speculative products where official surveys exist.

Score `yes` when participation expands beyond traditional buyers and the expansion contributes to price or credit momentum.

### 6. Broad Bullish Sentiment

Core question: is confidence in the boom broad across households, companies, lenders, investors, and public institutions?

Official indicators to seek:

- Consumer confidence, business confidence, and construction/real-estate confidence surveys.
- Central bank lending surveys showing strong loan demand and eased standards.
- IPO oversubscription, issuance volume, turnover ratio, equity fund account growth, or exchange activity where official.
- Regulator, central bank, or financial stability reports describing risk appetite, underwriting, or investor behavior.
- Official investor or household finance surveys when available.

Score `yes` when optimism is broad and risk concerns are widely discounted.

### 7. Tightening Risks Popping The Bubble

Core question: could higher rates, tighter credit, weaker currency, or regulation trigger deleveraging?

Official indicators to seek:

- Policy rate, money-market rates, long bond yields, real rates, and yield curve slope.
- Debt service ratios and share of floating-rate or short-maturity debt.
- Credit growth slowdown, lending standards, funding spreads, and non-performing loans.
- Current account balance, external debt, foreign-currency debt, reserves, exchange rate pressure, and capital flows.
- Macroprudential tightening, reserve requirements, loan caps, taxes, or regulatory restrictions.
- Central bank financial stability reports on stress-test sensitivity to rates, property prices, or exchange rates.

Score `yes` when plausible tightening can trigger falling asset prices, collateral pressure, forced selling, defaults, or a credit contraction.

## Reporting Guidance

Use short, evidence-heavy prose:

```text
Bubble call: Probable bubble. Five checks are yes and one is mixed. The strongest evidence is housing price-to-income at a record high, private credit rising faster than nominal GDP, investor mortgage growth, broad property optimism in official surveys, and high debt-service sensitivity to rate hikes. The main caveat is limited official data on forward purchases.
```

When sources disagree, explain the split:

```text
Equity valuation evidence is mixed: market-cap-to-GDP is high, but earnings growth partly offsets price gains. Housing evidence is stronger because price-to-income and price-to-rent ratios are both far above long-run averages.
```
