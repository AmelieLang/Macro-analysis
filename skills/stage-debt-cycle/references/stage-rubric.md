# Long-Term Debt Cycle Stage Rubric

## Core Idea

Classify a country-period by asking how borrowing, incomes, asset prices, debt service, and policy interact. The same country can transition gradually, and real-world data often straddle two adjacent stages.

Use this core sequence:

1. Early cycle.
2. Debt bubble.
3. Top.
4. Depression, also called recession/depression when the user uses that wording.
5. Reflationary deleveraging.

## Minimum Data Checklist

Prefer long history and quarterly or annual data:

- Total debt to GDP or debt to income, split by household, corporate, financial, and government if possible.
- Debt service as a percent of income or GDP.
- Nominal GDP or income growth; real GDP growth and productivity.
- Credit growth, credit standards, leverage, and shadow banking/non-bank credit.
- Equity prices and real estate prices, ideally relative to income, rents, earnings, or trend.
- Short-term and long-term interest rates; real rates when useful.
- Inflation, unemployment, bankruptcies/defaults, and banking stress.
- Fiscal balance, central bank policy, money/base money, QE, guarantees, bailouts, or capital controls.
- Currency and external debt stress for emerging markets or externally funded booms.

Use direction and rate of change. A high debt burden can be manageable in reflation, while a lower debt burden can be dangerous if income is collapsing and debt service is rising.

## Stage 1: Early Cycle

Core signature:
- Borrowing funds productivity-enhancing activity.
- Income growth is equal to or faster than debt growth.
- Debt-to-income or debt-to-GDP is stable or falling despite credit use.
- Balance sheets are healthy and debt-service ratios are manageable.
- Growth and inflation are neither too hot nor too cold.

Typical evidence:
- High real growth and productivity growth.
- Investment in capacity, technology adoption, infrastructure, education, exports, or industrial upgrading.
- Credit growth supports future income rather than pure asset purchases.
- Asset prices may rise, but not primarily because of leverage and speculative extrapolation.
- Central bank policy is not forced into repeated rate cuts to keep the expansion alive.

Red flags against early cycle:
- Debt-to-GDP is rising quickly.
- Asset returns depend on leverage and lower rates.
- Debt service rises faster than income.
- Credit standards loosen materially.

Example pattern:
- Japan after World War II through roughly 1970: rapid income and productivity growth without debt burdens rising materially relative to income.

## Stage 2: Debt Bubble

Core signature:
- Debt growth begins to outstrip income growth.
- Borrowing creates strong but increasingly unsustainable spending, income, and asset returns.
- Asset prices rise, collateral values rise, and lenders extend more credit.
- Credit standards weaken and leverage spreads across households, firms, financial institutions, or shadow banks.

Typical evidence:
- Debt-to-GDP rises materially and persistently.
- The typical large bubble may lever up by roughly 20 to 25 percentage points of GDP over about three years, though this is heuristic.
- Debt service rises even if rates have fallen, because debt stocks have grown.
- Asset valuations become expensive versus income, rents, earnings, or replacement cost.
- Markets become fully long, leveraged, and confident near the late bubble.
- Deregulation, financial innovation, cross-border capital inflows, or shadow banking can accelerate credit creation.
- Central banks often lower rates through repeated short-term cycles, requiring lower rates to revive each slowdown.

Early bubble versus late bubble:
- Early bubble: debt-to-income starts rising, but asset prices may still be partly justified by growth and lower rates.
- Late bubble: asset bubbles are obvious, leverage is broad, credit quality deteriorates, and speculative buyers dominate.

Red flags against debt bubble:
- Debt burdens are stable while income growth is strong.
- Asset prices are weak and credit is contracting.
- Policy is already openly reflating after a bust.

Example pattern:
- Japan from the 1970s into the late 1980s: slower productivity-led income potential plus financial deregulation and internationalization, with debt rising rapidly relative to incomes and a late-1980s asset bubble.

## Bubble Scoreboard

Use this scoreboard whenever the user asks whether there is a bubble, or whenever the country-period appears to be in the debt bubble or top stage. Score each question as `yes`, `no`, `mixed`, or `unknown`. A broad set of `yes` answers means the late-cycle bubble diagnosis is stronger.

The seven checks:

1. Are prices high relative to traditional measures?
2. Are prices discounting unsustainable conditions?
3. Are purchases being financed by high leverage?
4. Are buyers or companies making forward purchases?
5. Have new buyers or market participants entered the market?
6. Is there broadly bullish sentiment?
7. Does tightening risk popping the bubble?

### 1. Prices High Relative To Traditional Measures

Look for valuation excess relative to history and fundamentals:

- Equity prices versus earnings, book value, dividends, sales, replacement cost, or trend nominal GDP.
- Real estate prices versus rents, income, affordability, cap rates, or construction cost.
- Credit spreads, risk premia, and term premia that are too low for the macro and leverage backdrop.
- Country-specific valuation anchors where conventional ratios are distorted.

Score `yes` when prices are stretched across several traditional measures or far above the country's own history. Score `mixed` when only one market is stretched or fundamentals partly justify the move.

### 2. Prices Discounting Unsustainable Conditions

Ask what assumptions are embedded in prices:

- Growth, margins, rents, occupancy, commodity prices, or export demand must stay unusually strong.
- Rates, risk premia, defaults, or volatility must stay unusually low.
- Credit availability must remain easy for valuations to hold.
- Fiscal, currency, or policy conditions must remain benign despite rising imbalances.

Score `yes` when prices require extrapolating peak conditions. This often distinguishes a justified bull market from a bubble.

### 3. Purchases Financed By High Leverage

Look for leverage in the buyers and intermediaries:

- Margin debt, securities financing, repo, derivatives, structured products, or leveraged funds.
- Mortgage loan-to-value ratios, interest-only lending, speculative construction finance, or household leverage.
- Corporate borrowing for buybacks, M&A, capex with weak returns, or carry trades.
- Bank leverage, non-bank credit growth, shadow banking, foreign currency borrowing, or maturity mismatch.

Score `yes` when rising asset prices depend materially on borrowed money. Score `unknown` when leverage data are poor rather than assuming leverage is absent.

### 4. Forward Purchases By Buyers Or Companies

Forward purchases pull future demand into the present:

- Buyers purchase earlier than needed because they fear higher prices or scarce availability.
- Inventories, land banks, commodities, housing units, or capital goods are accumulated ahead of end demand.
- Companies build capacity, acquire assets, or buy financial assets based on extrapolated future demand.
- Households or firms borrow to lock in purchases before expected rate increases, tax changes, or price rises.

Score `yes` when demand appears accelerated from the future into the bubble period. This check is especially useful in property, commodity, and investment booms.

### 5. New Buyers Or Market Participants Entered

Late bubbles often broaden to marginal buyers:

- Retail investors, first-time property buyers, foreign buyers, or new speculative accounts enter aggressively.
- New funds, lenders, fintech platforms, securitization channels, or shadow banks expand the market.
- Previously conservative institutions change mandates to own the booming asset.
- Media, social pressure, or fear of missing out pulls new participants in.

Score `yes` when participation broadens beyond traditional buyers and when new channels increase credit supply.

### 6. Broadly Bullish Sentiment

Look for confidence that the boom is safe or structural:

- Widespread belief that prices cannot fall much or that a new era has begun.
- Low cash holdings, crowded positioning, high issuance appetite, easy covenant terms, or weak short interest.
- Narratives that dismiss valuation, leverage, or repayment risk.
- Policymakers, lenders, and investors become comfortable with ongoing prosperity.

Score `yes` when bullishness is broad across households, investors, lenders, companies, and public commentary. Score `mixed` if sentiment is strong in one segment but skepticism remains broad.

### 7. Tightening Risk Popping The Bubble

Ask whether the boom is vulnerable to tighter money or credit:

- Debt service is already high or rising, so rate increases hit cash flows quickly.
- Floating-rate debt, short maturities, foreign-currency debt, or refinancing needs are large.
- Credit growth and asset prices rely on low rates, low spreads, or easy funding.
- Central bank tightening, higher long rates, currency pressure, capital outflows, or regulatory tightening could force deleveraging.

Score `yes` when plausible tightening can trigger a self-reinforcing reversal. This question links the debt bubble to the top stage.

### Bubble Score Interpretation

Use this guide:

```text
6-7 yes: strong bubble evidence; likely late debt bubble or top depending on whether prices/rates have turned.
4-5 yes: probable bubble; classify as debt bubble if the up-wave continues, or top if tightening/price reversal has begun.
2-3 yes: watch for bubble conditions; do not overstate without stronger debt and asset evidence.
0-1 yes: weak bubble evidence unless core debt-cycle data strongly contradict the scoreboard.
unknown-heavy: lower confidence and list the missing data.
```

Override rules:

- Do not call a late bubble from valuations alone if leverage, new buyers, sentiment, and tightening vulnerability are absent.
- Do not ignore a bubble because one traditional valuation metric looks normal; use the full seven checks.
- If the scoreboard is strong and asset prices have started falling after tightening or a shock, consider `Top` rather than `Debt bubble`.
- If the scoreboard is strong and debt/income deterioration is already producing defaults and ineffective rate cuts, consider `Depression`.

Suggested output:

```text
Bubble scoreboard: 5/7 yes, 1 mixed, 1 unknown
Strongest checks: high valuations, leveraged buying, new participants, bullish sentiment, tightening vulnerability
Weakest/missing checks: forward purchases are unclear; traditional valuation evidence is mixed
Implication: probable late debt bubble; watch for transition to top if rates rise or asset prices roll over
```

## Stage 3: Top

Core signature:
- The bubble peaks and starts to break.
- Rising interest rates, tighter liquidity, or an exogenous shock triggers the turn.
- Asset prices roll over while debt burdens remain high.
- Borrowers and lenders discover that expected income and asset prices cannot validate the debt.
- The market is fully long, leveraged, and overpriced, so only modest tightening or a shock may be enough to reverse the boom.

Typical evidence:
- Equity or property market peak after a long leveraged run.
- Policy rates or long rates rise into stretched debt-service conditions.
- Credit spreads widen, funding conditions tighten, and risk appetite turns.
- New borrowing slows; defaults or delinquencies begin to rise.
- Debt service is near or past peak stress.
- Economic data may still look strong at first because tops often occur before the full downturn is visible.
- The yield curve flattens or inverts as short-term rates rise toward or above long-term rates, reducing the incentive to lend and increasing the incentive to hold cash.
- Vulnerable and frothy pockets show stress first: riskiest debtors miss payments, speculative borrowers lose funding, and fragile sectors start to crack while other sectors still look robust.
- Unemployment is often near cyclical lows and inflation is often rising, so central banks can continue tightening even as credit problems emerge.

Top triggers:
- Central bank tightening is the most common trigger, especially when growth and inflation are rising and capacity constraints are beginning to pinch.
- Tightening can be externally driven, such as by currency pressure, capital outflows, imported inflation, or global rate increases.
- Exogenous shocks can also trigger the top by hitting cash flows, collateral, confidence, or funding markets.

Transmission:
- Higher short rates raise debt-service costs, squeezing spending and weakening asset prices.
- Falling asset prices reduce collateral values and discourage additional lending and borrowing.
- Wider credit spreads and tighter lending standards move stress from the frothiest borrowers toward broader credit conditions.
- Runs from risky assets toward safer assets or cash can accelerate when the yield curve is flat or inverted.

Assessing bust magnitude:
- Focus less on the size of the rate increase alone and more on leverage, valuation excess, and sector sensitivity to tightening.
- Examine the largest sectors and players rather than relying only on economy-wide averages.
- Trace likely loss cascades: borrower cash-flow squeeze, collateral decline, lender loss, credit pullback, forced selling, income decline, and feedback into debt burdens.

Red flags against top:
- Asset prices are still early in a broad, productivity-led expansion with stable debt burdens.
- The bust is already deep and policy rates are near zero with defaults and unemployment surging, which points more to depression.
- Reflation has already restored income growth above debt growth.

Timing note:
- The equity-market top is a useful anchor in archetype charts. Around the top, debt service often peaks before or near the turn, while total debt-to-GDP may continue rising after the top because GDP/income falls.
- Japan late 1980s to early 1990s is a useful example: the Bank of Japan raised rates by roughly 4.5 percentage points from late 1988 to 1990 in response to asset inflation and overheating; activity slowed and equity/real-estate prices collapsed.

## Stage 4: Depression Or Recession/Depression

Core signature:
- The downturn becomes self-reinforcing.
- The same leverage linkages that inflated the bubble now work in reverse: asset prices fall, collateral falls, lending tightens, spending falls, incomes fall, and debt burdens worsen.
- Policymakers cut interest rates but cannot reverse the deflationary spiral with conventional easing alone.
- The affected markets are large and leveraged enough that losses on accumulated debt become systemically threatening.

Typical evidence:
- Asset prices decline sharply and remain impaired.
- Nominal income growth falls below debt growth; real growth contracts or stagnates.
- Debt-to-GDP can keep rising after the top because GDP/income falls even if new borrowing slows.
- Debt service may fall after rates are cut, but debt burdens can remain high.
- Defaults, bankruptcies, unemployment, and banking losses rise.
- Inflation falls, deflation appears, or the economy experiences debt-deflation pressure.
- Policy rates approach zero or the effective lower bound and lose traction.
- Fiscal deficits widen through automatic stabilizers, bailouts, and stimulus.
- Leveraged speculators are forced to sell as falling asset prices reduce equity and collateral values.
- Lenders, depositors, and investors withdraw from risky intermediaries and risky investments, producing liquidity runs.
- Risky intermediaries cannot meet withdrawals without asset sales or government support.
- Household debt relative to net worth can spike as asset values fall faster than debts are repaid or written down.
- Asset prices often fall dramatically from the cycle top; roughly 50% declines are common in archetypes, and 70-90% losses can occur in severe cases.

Self-reinforcing mechanics:
- Falling asset prices reduce collateral and net worth.
- Lower collateral causes lenders to pull back or demand more margin.
- Forced selling drives prices lower, causing more losses and more collateral pressure.
- Credit contraction cuts spending; one person's spending is another person's income.
- Falling incomes make existing debt harder to service and justify lower asset valuations.
- Debt write-downs reduce one party's liability but also reduce another party's asset, hurting balance sheets and demand.

Solvency versus cash-flow problems:
- `Solvency problem`: the entity lacks enough equity capital under accounting or regulatory rules and may need to be shut down, restructured, recapitalized, or nationalized.
- `Cash-flow problem`: the entity may have assets or capital but lacks cash to meet immediate withdrawals, margin calls, debt maturities, or operating needs.
- Most depression phases contain both. Cash-flow problems are often the immediate trigger because runs unfold faster than accounting recognition.

Why normal recession tools fail:
- In normal recessions, rate cuts can produce a positive wealth effect, stimulate activity, and ease debt-service burdens.
- In depressions, interest rates often cannot be cut enough because they are already near zero or the effective floor is higher due to currency, credit, or capital-flight risk.
- With monetary policy constrained, uncontrolled credit contraction can become an economic and social catastrophe unless policymakers offset deflationary forces with new forms of monetary/fiscal support.

Sector and player analysis:
- Identify the largest leveraged sectors, such as property, banks, shadow banks, households, commodity producers, developers, or externally indebted corporates.
- Estimate how losses cascade through lenders, collateral, deposits, wholesale funding, employment, fiscal balances, and private credit creation.
- Do not rely only on economy-wide averages; systemic risk often starts in concentrated, frothy pockets.

Red flags against depression:
- Income growth is already above debt growth and unemployment is falling.
- Debt burdens and debt service are declining while growth recovers.
- Asset prices are booming on fresh leverage rather than collapsing from prior excess.
- Rate cuts and liquidity support are clearly restoring credit creation without large defaults or restructurings.

Archetype note:
- In historical deflationary depressions, total debt as a share of GDP often peaks after the equity top, while debt service can peak earlier and then fall as rates are cut.
- The Great Depression and the post-Global Financial Crisis period both show policy rates falling to roughly zero while conventional cuts were insufficient to generate a self-reinforcing recovery.
- Japan after the late-1980s bubble is a useful example: stocks and real estate sold off by roughly 70%, banking losses became systemic, private credit creation stayed weak, deflation persisted, and debt burdens rose relative to income for years.

## Stage 5: Reflationary Deleveraging

Core signature:
- Policymakers shift from insufficient conventional easing to policies that reflate incomes.
- Income growth returns above debt growth.
- The economy rebounds while debt burdens and/or debt service decline.
- The deleveraging becomes more sustainable and less deflationary.

Typical evidence:
- Monetary and fiscal authorities deploy aggressive reflation: QE, base money expansion, guarantees, fiscal transfers, restructuring, recapitalization, yield control, or currency depreciation where applicable.
- Nominal GDP or income growth recovers above debt growth.
- Debt-to-GDP stabilizes or declines.
- Debt service falls as rates are low and income recovers.
- Asset prices recover, but ideally alongside income growth rather than pure speculative leverage.
- Unemployment improves and defaults/banking stress recede.
- Inflation moves away from deflation without becoming disorderly.

Beautiful versus ugly:
- Reflationary deleveraging can be beautiful if income growth, real growth, labor markets, and fiscal dynamics improve together.
- It can still be ugly if reflation relies on disorderly inflation, currency collapse, austerity, or prolonged stagnation.
- Use `classify-deleveraging-quality` when the user asks whether the deleveraging is beautiful or ugly.

Red flags against reflationary deleveraging:
- Policy is still only cutting rates without restoring income growth.
- Debt burdens rise because incomes keep falling.
- Asset recovery is not accompanied by income recovery.
- Inflation becomes disorderly and pushes rates or currency stress higher.

## Transition Patterns

Early cycle to debt bubble:
- Debt growth starts exceeding income growth.
- Productivity-led investment gives way to asset purchases, leverage, and weaker lending standards.

Debt bubble to top:
- Late-cycle leverage and asset prices become vulnerable to rising rates or shocks.
- Credit supply tightens and asset prices stop validating debt.

Top to depression:
- Asset declines hit collateral, lenders retreat, incomes fall, and debt burdens worsen.
- Conventional rate cuts fail to restart credit and spending.

Depression to reflationary deleveraging:
- Policymakers move beyond rate cuts.
- Income growth improves relative to debt growth and debt service falls.

## Scoring Aid

Score each category from -2 to +2, where positive scores point later toward reflation and negative scores point toward bubble/depression stress. Use judgment; do not add scores mechanically without checking the pattern.

```text
Debt burden trend:
  stable with strong income = early cycle
  rising faster than income = debt bubble
  very high near asset peak = top
  rising because income falls = depression
  stabilizing/falling with income recovery = reflationary deleveraging

Debt service:
  manageable = early cycle
  rising despite lower rates = debt bubble
  near peak stress = top
  falling due to cuts but debt burden still high = depression
  falling with income recovery = reflationary deleveraging

Asset prices:
  supported by productivity/income = early cycle
  leveraged boom = debt bubble
  peak/rollover = top
  bust = depression
  recovery after policy pivot = reflationary deleveraging

Rates and policy:
  normal policy room = early cycle
  progressively lower rates needed = debt bubble
  tightening/shock trigger = top
  rates near zero and ineffective = depression
  fiscal/monetary reflation restores income = reflationary deleveraging
```

## Common Misclassifications

- Calling every high-debt country a depression. High debt can coexist with reflation if income growth exceeds debt growth.
- Calling every equity decline a top. A top requires prior bubble conditions and tightening/shock dynamics.
- Calling strong growth an early cycle when debt-to-income is rising quickly and asset prices are leveraged.
- Calling rate cuts reflationary deleveraging before income growth exceeds debt growth.
- Ignoring currency and external debt in emerging markets, where the cycle can turn through balance-of-payments stress before domestic rates reach zero.

## Output Guidance

Use concise, evidence-backed language:

- "This period looks like the debt bubble stage: debt is rising faster than income, asset prices are strong, leverage is broadening, and policy is repeatedly easing to sustain growth."
- "This period is best classified as the top: asset prices have rolled over after a leveraged boom, rates have tightened, and debt-service stress is visible while the macro downturn has not fully played out."
- "This period is depression moving toward reflationary deleveraging: conventional rate cuts failed, but policy has pivoted to reflation and nominal income is beginning to outrun debt growth."
