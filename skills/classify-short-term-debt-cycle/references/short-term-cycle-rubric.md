# Archetypical Short-Term Debt Cycle Rubric

## Core Idea

Classify the country-period by reading how central bank policy, credit, slack, inflation, debt service, spending, production, labor, and assets interact. The archetypical short-term debt cycle has six phases:

1. Early recovery.
2. Mid-cycle.
3. Late cycle.
4. Late boom/early recession.
5. Early recession.
6. Late recession.

The cycle turns when rate cuts or rate hikes change borrowing, debt service, asset prices, and spending. One person's spending is another person's income, so the expansion and contraction both become self-reinforcing.

## Minimum Indicator Checklist

Core indicators:

- Growth versus potential.
- GDP gap.
- Inflation.
- Unemployment.
- Short-term rate.
- Yield curve.
- Private debt/GDP.
- Housing.
- Durables consumption.
- Capacity utilization.
- Real equity prices.

Useful market and activity additions:

- Industrial production.
- Retail sales.
- Business fixed investment.
- Corporate profits and margins.
- Commodity prices and inflation-hedge assets.
- Bond yields and bond total returns.
- Credit growth, delinquencies, lending standards, and debt service.

Use these official or reliable series when assessing the United States:

## Phase 1: Early Recovery

Core signature:
- The economy is emerging from recession.
- Activity rises from depressed levels.
- Slack is high: idle factories, high unemployment, underemployment, and weak hours worked.
- Inflation is low, falling, or not yet a problem.
- The central bank is stimulative, keeping rates low and cutting as needed.
- Rate cuts trigger new borrowing and a wealth effect from higher asset prices.

Typical evidence:
- Real growth rebounds from recession lows but output remains below potential.
- GDP gap is negative.
- Unemployment is elevated but starts to fall or flatten.
- Credit growth speeds up from depressed levels.
- Housing, autos, and other interest-rate-sensitive durables improve first.
- Retail sales and inventories recover as consumers and businesses restock.
- Capacity utilization rises from low levels.
- Business investment is still sluggish but begins improving.
- Inflation is flat or falling because slack remains large.

Markets:
- Equities usually rally strongly because recessionary expectations were discounted and rates are low/falling.
- Bond yields are flat or down.
- Commodities and inflation-hedge assets are usually weak because inflation and capacity pressure are low.

Box 1 pattern:
- Equities rally.
- Bond yields flat.
- Short rates fall.
- Commodities flat/down.
- Consumption flat/expanding.
- Debt bottoms and starts to increase.
- Capacity utilization flat/down then improves.
- Inflation flat/falls.
- Housing and consumer durables expand.
- Employment falls/flat, then stabilizes.

Timing:
- Often lasts about 18 months, but varies by recession depth and policy response.

## Phase 2: Mid-Cycle

Core signature:
- The initial recovery spurt moderates.
- Growth hums along, not too hot and not too cold.
- Slack remains meaningful.
- Inflation remains low or stable.
- The central bank does not need to tighten materially.

Typical evidence:
- Real growth is steady and near expectations.
- GDP gap remains negative or near zero but closing gradually.
- Unemployment continues improving.
- Credit and consumption expand at a healthier pace.
- Capacity utilization rises but is not constrained.
- Housing and durables rise or flatten after early-cycle surge.
- Inflation stabilizes.

Markets:
- Equity gains taper as growth comes in near expectations.
- Bond yields rise modestly or stay flat.
- Inflation-hedge asset declines slow as inflation stabilizes.
- Commodities may rise modestly as demand improves.

Box 2 pattern:
- Equities rally/flat.
- Bond yields rise.
- Short rates flat/up.
- Commodities up.
- Consumption expands.
- Debt rises.
- Capacity utilization rises.
- Inflation flat/rises.
- Housing and consumer durables rise/flat.
- Employment expands.

Timing:
- Often lasts about a year, but depends on slack, early-cycle growth speed, and disinflationary forces.

## Phase 3: Late Cycle

Core signature:
- Persistent above-potential growth consumes excess capacity.
- Inflation pressures build.
- The economy approaches capacity limits while money and credit spent grows faster than goods and services produced.
- Companies struggle to meet demand; unemployment is low; wages and input prices rise.

Typical evidence:
- Real growth remains strong, often above potential.
- GDP gap approaches or exceeds zero.
- Capacity utilization passes average levels and becomes constrained.
- Unemployment is low and labor bargaining power rises.
- Inflation and inflation expectations trend higher.
- Business fixed investment accelerates.
- Corporate profits can be temporarily exaggerated by investment-boom accounting effects: sellers book capital goods revenue immediately while buyers expense costs over time.
- Credit increasingly finances goods, services, and financial assets.
- Debt service begins rising as debts and rates rise.

Markets:
- Inflation-hedge assets and commodities often become the best performers.
- Bond yields rise as investors demand inflation compensation.
- Equities can continue rising on strong demand, high profits, and optimism.
- Asset prices become more vulnerable to reversal as debt service and rates rise.

Box 3 pattern:
- Equities fall or become vulnerable after late strength.
- Bond yields flat.
- Short rates up.
- Commodities flat/up.
- Consumption growth peaks/falls.
- Debt growth slows.
- Capacity constrained, investment high.
- Inflation high.
- Housing and consumer durables decline.
- Employment above capacity; growth peaks.

Bubble note:
- Short-cycle bubbles can occur in this phase. They are often smaller than late long-term debt-cycle bubbles but can still cause pain.

## Phase 4: Late Boom/Early Recession

Core signature:
- The economy is in the last stages of the upturn.
- Unemployment is low, spending exceeds productive capacity, and prices rise rapidly.
- The central bank tightens more aggressively to offset inflation pressure.
- Higher rates slow debt growth and spending.

Typical evidence:
- Short rates rise materially.
- Yield curve flattens or inverts as long rates price future easing.
- Liquidity falls and cash becomes more attractive relative to risky or long-duration assets.
- Debt service on new and variable-rate debt rises.
- Housing, consumer durables, and other rate-sensitive sectors weaken.
- Equities, bonds, real estate, and other long-duration assets fall as discount rates rise and the outlook dims.
- The expansion ends as the central bank hits the brakes.

Markets:
- Equities begin to sell off.
- Bond yields may fall as growth expectations deteriorate even while short rates remain high.
- Commodities and inflation hedges can initially stay firm but become vulnerable.
- Cash becomes relatively attractive.

Policy:
- The yield curve is often most tight in this part of the cycle.

## Phase 5: Early Recession

Core signature:
- The central bank sacrifices growth and stays tight even as the economy deteriorates.
- It takes time to create slack sufficient to bring inflation back under control.
- Spending falls, incomes fall, credit problems emerge, and the contraction becomes self-reinforcing.

Typical evidence:
- Real growth slows or contracts.
- Credit growth falls sharply, sometimes by about half in archetypical cycles.
- Households cut spending because wealth is down, debt service is high, and new borrowing is expensive.
- Lenders pull back as incomes fall and weaker borrowers struggle.
- Business investment collapses as companies see excess capacity relative to future spending.
- Production slows and layoffs begin.
- Unemployment rises substantially.
- Fiscal deficit expands through unemployment insurance and automatic stabilizers.
- Inflation may still be high enough to delay immediate easing.
- Yield curve flattens further or inverts: short rates remain high while bond yields fall on expected future easing.

Markets:
- Equities decline sharply, often around 20% in archetypical cycles.
- Commodities and inflation-hedge assets collapse as demand weakens.
- Bonds can rally if long rates fall and credit risk is contained.

Box 4 pattern:
- Equities flat/fall.
- Bond yields down.
- Short rates flat/down only after staying tight.
- Commodities down.
- Consumption falls.
- Savings rise.
- Capacity utilization falls.
- Inflation falls.
- Housing and consumer durables down/flat.
- Employment falls.

Policy:
- The central bank may not cut immediately because a quick return to strong growth could rekindle inflation.
- This painful phase can be necessary to create slack after overheating.

## Phase 6: Late Recession

Core signature:
- Inflation pressures finally subside.
- The central bank lowers rates enough to counter the downward spiral.
- The bottom in economic activity forms.
- Equity markets often turn before the economy because lower discount rates lift present values.

Typical evidence:
- Inflation falls materially from highs, often by several percentage points in archetypical cycles.
- Short rates are cut aggressively; average easing can be large, around 2.5 percentage points in archetypes.
- Easing often continues for an extended period.
- Debt service costs fall.
- New borrowing becomes more attractive.
- Asset prices rise and create a positive wealth effect.
- Households with stable balance sheets begin borrowing for homes, autos, and other big-ticket items.
- Broader economy remains weak; hiring and business investment lag until demand recovery is sustained.

Markets:
- Equities bottom and rally before macro data fully recover.
- Bonds perform well as rates fall.
- Commodities may remain weak until demand turns.
- Housing and durables start stabilizing first.

Transition:
- Low inflation, low rates, and a rallying stock market lay the groundwork for the next early recovery.

## Phase Scoring

Score each phase from 0 to 2:

```text
2 = most core indicators match the phase
1 = mixed evidence or transition
0 = phase is contradicted
unknown = insufficient data
```

Pick the highest-scoring phase. If two adjacent phases tie, label `Mixed/transition` and explain which data would break the tie.

High-confidence calls require evidence from at least four areas:
- Growth/slack.
- Inflation/labor.
- Rates/yield curve.
- Credit/housing/durables.
- Markets.

## Common False Reads

- Calling early recovery just because equities rally. Confirm slack, low inflation, low rates, and improving credit-sensitive spending.
- Calling mid-cycle when inflation and capacity utilization are already rising into constraint.
- Calling late cycle from strong growth alone. Confirm slack is gone, inflation pressure is rising, and rates/yields are moving up.
- Calling recession too early because equities fall. Confirm tightening, curve behavior, credit slowdown, and activity deterioration.
- Calling late recession before inflation falls enough for aggressive easing.

## Output Guidance

Use concise, evidence-backed phrasing:

- "This looks like early recovery: rates are low, slack is still large, inflation is falling, equities have rallied, and housing/durables are responding to easier credit."
- "This looks like late cycle: growth has run above potential long enough to close the output gap, unemployment is low, capacity utilization is high, inflation is rising, and rates/yields are moving up."
- "This looks like early recession: the central bank remains tight, the curve is inverted, activity and credit are weakening, equities and commodities are down, and unemployment is starting to rise."
